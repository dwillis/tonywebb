LEICESTER DAILY POST: MONDAY 17 JUNE 1895 - 1.

SPORTS AND PASTIMES.

Notts have paid off one of their old scores with Leicestershire at last, and very decisively too. This may be accounted for to a certain extent by the lace county playing on their own green, and to no indifferent batting on the part of the visitors. The last time these counties met at Nottingham was on June 10, 1878, when Notts won by 10 wickets, and on Wednesday last they were victorious by an innings and 16 runs. But both teams have greatly altered since the former encounter, and the only man in the Notts team at the present time who took part in that match is Wilfrid Flowers, who is still one of the mainstays of his county. That he can still hold his own with the present-day batsmen was shown in the Yorkshire and Notts match, in which he was responsible for 119, and actually saved his side from defeat. I must now hark back to the match.

The old argument again stood good that our bowling was considerably stronger than our defence, but probably in a measure the ill-luck which attends Mr. de Trafford has something to do with it. Time after time have great things been anticipated from this player, but up to the present this season he has only flattered to deceive. The wicket was a perfect one, and Mr. de Trafford winning the toss made the Notts players shake their heads as if to denote that they were in for a long spell of leather hunting, and this looked highly probable at one time, though the captain left when but a few runs were scored. Mr. Hillyard, Holland, and Tomlin, the latter of whom gave a fine display, raised the hopes of the few followers of the team, but they were blighted just as quickly, for the usual rot set in, and Chapman, who was playing a la Barlow, had no one to assist him but Whiteside.

Notts were in a very similar predicament to that of Leicestershire, as it was a case of two men obtaining the runs—A. O. Jones and Gunn. That the amateur deserves great credit for his century I admit, but had the chance offered so early in Gunn's innings been accepted the probability is he would have been minus his proud distinction, for, as events proved, he would have had no one to remain with him.

At the conclusion of Tuesday's play and on Wednesday morning rain fell heavily, and matters went so badly that six wickets were down for the beggarly total of 35, and "Our Dick" actually bagged a brace. But Messrs. Hillyard and Lorrimer "hit away" and forced the game to such an extent that Notts looked like having to go in a second time. This I should have very much liked to have seen, for they would have had a troublesome time with Pougher, Woodcock, and Co., and to use the words of one of the Notts players, "If they had been set a hundred they would never have got them." Exception must be taken to the bowling changes of Mr. De Trafford. Under such circumstances as the long partnership between Jones and Gunn many captains would have had every man on in the team that had any pretensions to bowling, and yet Chapman, who, if my memory serves me right, broke such a partnership on the County Ground, Tomlin, and Holland never bowled a ball. It often happens that the worst of bowling has the desired effect, and nothing is lost by a trial.

The match which followed against the Dublin University, excepting financially, was a gratifying success, and Mr. Hulyard is to be congratulated, as owing to his wisdom Leicestershire can now claim a triumph instead of a drawn game. What a happy inspiration that must have been to have closed the innings when he did! The game from beginning to end was an eventful one, and not until the close of the second innings of the home county could it guaged with any certainty. Then a draw seemed most probable. Although our County Committee will lose considerably by the venture, they will be doubly repaid by the gratifying success of Knight. I have noticed the correspondence advocating that this young player should have a further trial, and the committee being fully alive to the necessity of strengthening the batting of the team thought this a very favourable opportunity. Knight's success in this match only bears out my argument in a previous note, that his talent was being completely wasted at Melton. He has now proved what a sterling bat he is, and he should be played regularly, so as to gain that confidence which is so essential in the game of cricket.

The 'Varsity captain won the toss, and considering his side would have an advantage in fielding, owing to the surface of the ground being slightly on the soft side, put Leicestershire in to bat. No doubt had Mr. Hillyard been so favoured with the spin he would have adopted the same course under the circumstances. Thanks, however, to the careful batting of Holland and Chapman this was overcome, and when Mr. Hillyard and Tomlin were in everything went as smoothly as the proverbial wedding bells. Mr. Hillyard fairly excelled himself in batting, and but for Pougher being unfortunately run out he most probably would have beaten all previous scores for his county. This apparently upset Mr. Hillyard, and he was never comfortable after. There were not many people present, but those that were there eagerly watched every stroke made by Knight, and real good strokes they were. It was also gratifying to see the improvement in Mr. D. Lorrimer.

What the Irishmen would have done without the score of D. H. Gwynn I don't know, for of the remainder A. W. Porter was the only one who played the bowling with any confidence, and he should have been caught before he had scored. But that's just where the luck of the game comes in, and if it were not for these mistakes we should not have so many big scores. This, however, was nothing to be compared to the luck which smiled on the Irish captain, as he was missed on no fewer than four occasions, and very singularly by two men.

In the second innings of Leicestershire we were favoured with the best display of cricket yet seen on the ground this season. Holland and Knight gave a very pretty display, but this was fairly eclipsed when Tomlin set to work, and I fancy the Irishmen will take back with them a favourable impression of his smiling powers. He banged the bowling to all parts of the ground, and those who saw it can just imagine his performance at the Oval. Pougher also batted exceedingly well for his 50 not out, and D. Lorrimer maintained his improvement.

On Thursday afternoon, playing for the Post-office against the Tradesmen, on the Victoria Park, C. Luck scored 98, and was doing his utmost to reach the coveted three figures when he had the misfortune to play the ball on to his wicket. He had, however, two or three very palpable lives.

The matches on the Park on Saturday afternoon were of a moderate class, and in the absence of players of repute, attention was more evenly distributed than is generally the case. The South End gave a tame exhibition of batting against Belgrave's bowling, S. A. Walker securing six wickets at a cost of six runs each; while Smith had three for 23. For the South End, the old Leicestershire professional, Rylott, was playing, and met with a hearty reception. He still bowls very effectively, but at one time in the match received considerable punishment from Richards, who was responsible for no less than 40 runs out of the 74 aggregated. Matthews and Rylott had four wickets each, the former maintaining a splendid length, and being almost unplayable.

Dan Garner's met a team going by the name of Geo. Oliver's, but as a matter of fact it was made up of members of the Ivanhoe, Roslyn, and Belvoir, all of which were without games, only three of Oliver's taking part. All the bowling execution was done by Pougher and Hings, of the Belvoir and Roslyn respectively, and owing to an excellent start by Ward and Hill, continued by Lovett and Sutton, Garner's set their opponents a difficult task, for the total reached 185. As drawing of stumps this was being rapidly approached, three wickets falling for 100, 46 of which Coleman quickly put together.

The Temperance received a big thrashing from London-road, who, first aggregating 192, disposed of their teetotal friends for 81. To the winning score Cheshire (another Roslyn player) contributed 98. He went in first, and played right through the innings, losing his tenth partner just as the century seemed a certainty. The innings was a most meritorious one, for few liberties were taken, and he was but once missed.

Exton scored 63 not out for the Sparkenhoe, which club made 111 for the loss of one wicket in response to St. Matthew's 130; A. Gunton notched 55 for J. and R. Morley's, who drew with R. Walker and Sons; A. Bailey was not out 53 for Hill-street against B. Ellis and Co.; the Brothers Hlston were mainly responsible for an easy victory by the Oxford over the Ibex, D. Hlston scoring 62, while from his brother's bat came 46 runs, and he took seven wickets for but 15 runs; Thurmaston defeated Wycliffe by 3 to 42; the chief feature of the match being the splendid trundling of Stinson, who captured eight wickets for 15 runs; and for the Secular, who defeated J. Richardson's, Langham took four for 11.

I am very pleased to hear that E. Tomlin, brother of W. Tomlin, the county professional, has made such a favourable impression at Oxford, where he is fulfilling an engagement. He has been bowling at such players as G. J. Mordaunt, P. F. Warner, and H. K. Foster, and many others of note. This is a big recommendation for young Tomlin.

Tomlin. A friend writes to me to the effect that S. M. J. Woods has approached one of the professionals there with a view to his playing for Somersetshire, and unless his own county play him he is very likely to have the option of playing for that county. His engagement terminates at Oxford next week.

Although Leicestershire are without a match during the next three days, their players will be well represented in the M.C.C. team that will oppose Hampshire at Lord's to-day. I heard after the match at Aylestone on Saturday that Pougher, Woodcock, Whiteside and Gesson were selected to play. Their achievements no doubt will be interesting to their many admirers at home.

The June number of "Form at a Glance" is to hand, with racing fully indexed up to date. Making its appearance just on the eve of the Royal Ascot meeting, it is doubly welcome. The publishers are J. G. Hammond and Co., Moor-street, Birmingham.

After the close of each cricket season the committee of the M.C.C. shall decide the county championship.
One point shall be reckoned for each win; one deducted for each loss; unfinished games shall not be reckoned.
The county which during the season shall have, in finished matches, obtained the greatest proportionate number of points shall be reckoned champion county.
