LEIGHTON v. OXFORD HARLEQUINS.
The annual match between these teams took place on the Leighton ground on Monday and Tuesday. On the first day the game was witnessed by upwards of 1,000 spectators. This number was far in excess of the attendance at last year's match, and as practically the same county players, with the exception of Ranjitsinhji, were in the field on the last occasion, it may justly be assumed that the increased attendance was due to the inclusion in the Leighton team of this popular young Indian player, who by his brilliant and consistent play, both with the bat and in the field, has gained for himself the sobriquet of the "Indian Grace of Cricket." Mr. Laverton had strengthened his team, not only by the services of Ranjitsinhji, but by Brain, the Sussex county bowler, and Trask, one of the Somerset reserves, while Fry, the Oxford triple blue, who last year played for Leighton, transferred his allegiance to the visitors. The Harlequins brought a very strong team, including five of the Somerset county team, no doubt resolving to avenge the defeat they suffered at the hands of Leighton last year. Glorious cricketing weather prevailed throughout the day, and nothing further could be wished for in this respect. Newman had prepared a splendid pitch, and with a hard and true wicket it was inevitable that high scoring would be witnessed. The spin of the coin was in Mr. Laverton's favour, and, electing to send the visitors to the field, F. W. Stancomb and W. Trask went to the wicket just on the stroke of 12, Fry and Cassan taking the ball. Stancomb played a very steady and valuable game, defending the wickets for nearly two hours for 24 runs, being ultimately caught at short leg by L. Palairet. Ranjitsinhji took first wicket down. His play was undoubtedly the feature of the game. In an exceedingly graceful and faultless style, and with some especially pretty hits to leg, he won the admiration of every cricketer on the ground, and it was very hard lines that after putting together 19 he was run out by Brain from Rose, the ball bounding on to the wicket from the wicket-keeper's guards. Brain contributed a useful 35, his innings being a rather lucky one, as he was several times missed in the field. Coxeter had the honour of compiling the highest score. He played dashing cricket for his innings of 61, his fine cuts being especially admired. His score included a six (which included three overthrows), five fours, two threes, and two twos. The only other men to reach double figures were Shorland and Stancomb, the former scoring 15 by some hard hitting, and the latter making some pretty cuts for four and three in his score of 17. The innings closed soon after four for 239. The fielding on the whole was good, but there was an extraordinary large number of extras, owing to Brain, the wicket-keeper, standing in close to the fast bowling. Cassan's tricky bowling was by far the most effective, and he succeeded in taking six wickets for 70 runs. The visitors commenced their innings at 4.20. R. C. N. Palairet and Hill went first to the wickets, while Trask and Atfield were put on to bowl. Runs came very quickly, but Palairet was soon caught in the slips by Shorland, the telegraph board showing 20-1-14. His score included some pretty cuts. Hill was joined by Fry, and these two quickly made matters lively, and Laverton found it advisable to augment the long field. Some slashing cricket was witnessed, and bowlers were frequently changed, but without the desired effect. Hill was badly missed at the wicket by F. W. Stancomb when he had scored 21, and Fry, too, was similarly favoured before he had reached 40. A. G. Stancomb mustering a beautiful catch in the long field. Hill passed his century by a fine cut to long leg, having been batting just over an hour. Ranjitsinhji, however, ultimately succeeded in doing the needful. Hill, tempted by a simple straight ball, jumped out, and attempted to pull to leg, with the result that he missed and his stumps were shattered. Throughout his innings he played in slashing style, his score of 115 including one six, nine fours, nine threes, 13 twos. Fry was now joined by Palairet, and these two entering partnership at 6.15 carried the score to 199 before play was suspended for the day.
Ideal weather also prevailed on Tuesday, and there was again a large attendance of spectators, who throughout showed great interest in the game, cheering impartially anything extraordinary smart on the part of the players, irrespective to which side they belonged. The wicket was even faster than on the previous day, and consequently some grand scoring was witnessed. Trask and Atfield were again put on to bowl. Palairet took the first ball, with the result that the double century was reached. Newman, one of the umpires, had to decide a knotty question early in the play. Fry, in defending his wickets, hit the ball twice, the second time playing it in the wicket-keeper's hands. The bowler appealed, and the umpire decided in favour of the batsman, but opinion was freely expressed that the decision was an erroneous one. Under-hand John Newnham puzzled the batsmen, but they soon got used to it, and it was evident they were settled for a long partnership. The bowlers were changed in quick succession, but still the score rapidly increased, and when at length Fry's wicket fell to Atfield the telegraph board read—355-3-163. Fry at times was in brilliant form, and some very smart hits to boundaries were witnessed. His score was composed of one six, 19 fours, six threes, and 16 twos. Rose filled the vacancy, and 400 was reached just before one o'clock. Roe's stay at the wickets was very short, for having scored 15, including a tremendous hit into the next field, he was caught at long-off by Laverton, who brought off a really grand catch. Hiran came in next, and soon after Palairet was caught at mid-off by Ranjitsinhji, the ball spinning in the air off the edge of the bat. Dunlop was the new comer, but Ranjitsinhji at mid-off was again successful, this time Dunlop being the victim from a ball by Atfield. Brain, however, failed to take the lesson to heart, and Atfield, sending down a similar ball, he hit out in precisely the same manner as his late partner, the result being that Ranjitsinhji, over on the alert for the third time in succession brought off a very difficult catch, taking the ball high up with his left hand—a performance which provoked a round of applause. 446 was recorded at lunch time for the loss of eight wickets, and L. C. H., thinking there was a possibility of dismissing the opposing team for a smaller sum, declared the innings closed. Leighton commenced their second attempt just after two o'clock, Stancomb and Trask taking the last against the bowling of Fry and Cassan. The former, having smartly scored 21, was dismissed by a splendid catch by Hill at long leg. Ranjitsinhji again took first wicket down, his appearance from the pavilion being greeted with a hearty round of applause, and it is very evident that he had won the good opinions of the people of this part of the world. For two hours and forty minutes he played a faultless game. Commencing very cautiously he soon warmed to his work, and boundary followed boundary in quick succession. It was seldom that he made a faulty stroke; there were absolutely no lofty hits, but good crisp strokes all along the carpet. The frequent changes in the bowling did not trouble him, and Cassan's tricky bowling and Fry's tremendous fast ones did not disconcert him in the least. He was eventually smartly caught by Brain at point, the batsman endeavouring to send to boundary over the fieldsmen's head. It is almost needless to add that he received an ovation on returning to the pavilion. His score was composed of 21 fours, two threes, and 14 twos. More consistent scoring was shown by the remainder of the team, but after "Ranji's" disappearance from the scene of action all interest was gone in the game. The total reached 331, so that in the two innings Leighton made 570 for 18 wickets, while their opponents compiled 446 for eight wickets. It will be seen from these figures that no less than 1,016 runs were scored during the two days, and in this respect a new record was made, the highest number of runs made previously in any one match being just over 900. Although the visitors had undoubtedly the best of the match the home team on the whole played a sterling game, their weakest point being certainly in the fielding, and in this respect they were decidedly inferior to the visitors. If the chances given by Hill, Fry, and Palairet before they had scored very high had been successfully brought off the match would have assumed a totally different complexion, for the visitors' total, shorn of the three centuries, would have looked insignificant in comparison of their opponents. It will be noticed with gratification that Atfield, the popular Leighton pro., secured the best bowling average, notwithstanding the hard luck he had in the field.
