STOURBRIDGE COUNTY EXPRESS: SATURDAY 10 AUGUST 1895-2.
NOTES.
Owing to the rain on Saturday, the greater number of the games set for decision in the district had to be postponed or else were stopped after very little play had been indulged in. One result of the downpour was to soften the wicket, giving the bowlers an advantage for the time being; though, after the second downpour the ball became so greasy that the trundlers could do nothing with it, and the batsmen again assumed the ascendancy. Such, at any rate, was the effect produced at Cape Hill, where a friendly game between Stourbridge and Mitchell's was being played.
Stourbridge had but a very poor team on, Collis, Foster, Rhodes, Foley and Arnold all being away, so that the eleven was far from representative. Notwithstanding that, a good show was made, and when wickets were drawn, the game was in a delightfully uncertain phase, though, but for the second shower, there need be no hesitancy in saying that the visitors would have won. As it stood, however, there was a possibility that Stour would have won, for despite the fearful state of the ball J. Hingley was bowling a splendid length, getting a little "work" on as well, which frequently beat the batsmen. He finished up with 3 for 18, which was decidedly good under the circumstances.
Good though this was, T. Stevenson surpassed it by taking 4 wickets for 37 runs, which included the "hat trick," Fereday, Punchard, and Harrison being out to successive balls. There was nothing very striking about the batting, though, to be correct, perhaps some people would say the batting was all striking. But remarkable would probably be the better word. Fereday, aided by a "life" or two played patient cricket for 39, this including three 4's and three 3's. In his 23, J. Hingley smote one 4, two 3's, and four two's. This was one of the pleasant features about the game—to see him making runs in something like his old style. This season he appears so far to be unable to do very well with either bat or ball.
Another feature about the game was that four wickets fell with the total at 86. G. Stevenson, 20 (not out) contributed, it will be seen, five-ninths of the home team's total, and by very decent cricket too. The scores, when Jupiter P. put his veto on the game were Stourbridge 83, Mitchell's 38 for 4 wickets.
Losing the toss meant a vast difference to Staffordshire in their return match against Worcestershire on Monday and Tuesday, at Amblecote. It is an almost incontrovertible fact that, in many instances, and under certain circumstances, winning the toss means winning the game. I think it did so in the game under notice; though, at the same time, the utter collapse of the visitors in the second innings was far more complete than anyone anticipated.
When the game started on Monday, Worcestershire cut a very sorry figure; indeed with such men as Arnold, H. K. Foster, P. H. Latham, Millward, and Bowley out for 33, matters did not seem much rosier for them than they did for Staffordshire later on. A change occurred from the partnership of the brothers Martin, who just reached the half century before being parted. Then one of those changes, which are the very essence of the game, took place. E. P. Jobson partnered K. G. Brumley-Martin, and a stand of over 70 runs ensued, putting quite a different aspect on the state of affairs. Mr. Martin batted stylishly for just half-a-hundred, an innings of great value to his side, being made just when they were wanted.
It was an innings such as those which delight the eye and the heart of those who see it. Those crisp late cuts and the hard driving and sound defence making his innings alike attractive and pleasurable.
Mr. Jobson was like King Richard, "himself again," and it was unfortunate for the Staffordshire bowlers that they found him in such a happy vein. His play was quite up to the best form he ever showed, and it is questionable if, all the years he has been in the cricket arena, he has played any better innings than that of Monday. To go in seventh wicket down with the score at 50, and carry out his bat for 83 not out, out of the 140 odd runs put on is a splendid performance. He never gave the semblance of a chance, he hit all round the wicket freely, cutting and driving with especial severity, one straight hit out of the ground being very fine. A better display one could not wish to see. He had a little difficulty with one or two balls. Generally speaking, however, he was quite at home, his variety of strokes standing him in good stead and helping to deal with all the insidious attacks at dislodging him from his position. When he seemed certain to reach three figures, his partner, Raynor, by a piece of bad judgment, ran himself out, else there is no doubt whatever he would have been credited with the coveted century.
After the inauspicious opening of the innings, it was decidedly good to finish up with the substantial total of 192. Thanks chiefly to the rain, and a bit of sunshine on Tuesday afternoon, this aggregate proved altogether beyond the powers of Staffordshire to equal in their two ventures. P. Brigs, 20 and 9, was top scorer in both innings, which were devoid of interest except from the fact that they brought out some wonderful analyses. On the first occasion Bird (A) came out with four for 18, and Raynor four for 18, and Arnold two for 22. These were easily eclipsed in the next round, so to speak. Arnold simply made the ball do what he wanted, while Raynor's expresses absolutely pulverised the visiting team. Arnold finished up with five for 9, and Raynor five for 12, which with two extras filled up the 23 made by the whole side.
A paltry total, indeed, even though the wicket was in favour of the leather handlers. The only explanation of the complete breakdown seems to be an utter inability to cope with the bowling, perhaps accentuated by a bit of funk. To be beaten by an innings and 83 runs shows how immeasurably superior the bowlers of Worcestershire were to the batsmen of Staffordshire, and I say this without losing sight of the fact that perhaps the positions may have been altered—I don't go so far as to say the visitors would have won—had they won the toss instead of losing it.
The win does not alter the position of Worcestershire in the competition, as they still remain second, Durham being the leaders.
A good deal of surprise was manifested at the meagre gates. Considering how strong Worcestershire are this season in every department, there was some room for it; but it should not be forgotten that there were great counter attractions in and around the district which probably accounted for the paucity of the attendance. I think the committee have been well pleased with the result of previous county matches here; therefore it is hoped that the poor financial return of the game under notice will not deter them from introducing the district to county cricket again next season, when, let us hope, a date will be chosen on which there will be no counter attractions to tell against the attendance. Worcestershire county cricket is in the ascendancy, and it only requires the public's support to prevent any retrograde movement; it is much easier to come down the ladder of county cricket than to get up. It is necessary that liberal support should be forthcoming, so that the committee may be enabled to qualify and obtain such men as will add further lustre to the county's name, now that there is such a revival of their once flagging fortunes.
Arnold's play was the theme of conversation among the Staffordshire men, and one of them prophesied "He'll be among the tip toppers presently." Arnold was at that moment fairly slaughtering the Staffordshire batsmen. With a year or two more over his head and additional stamina he should make a great name on the cricket field.
At the Kidderminster Weavers' Sports on Monday G. A. Noks, Stourbridge Junior Harriers, from the 85 yards mark, ran away with the mile handicap. Another sport for the juniors. At Sport and Play tournament F. W. Baker landed the quarter and half-mile races for unlicensed riders. At Kidderminster, on Monday, the brothers Squires landed a second and third, and at Wolverhampton, on Tuesday, Charles a third, his brother just beating him for second place. Spearn, riding at Kidderminster on Monday, credited himself with the half-mile open.
Pretty good list for Stourbridge that.
Belbroughton were robbed by the rain of another victory on Saturday. Blakedown were at Belbroughton, and winning the toss sent in the home team, who, aided by splendid batting by J. Houghton (32) and W. Darby (30 not out) made 92 for nine wickets. Replying to this the "Downs" contributed 31 for six wickets, the gently falling rain then terminating the match. Hard lines, "Bella."
What a crushing defeat Himley inflicted upon St. James', Dudley. The Saints made 29, 12 of which came from the effort of Gould, and 7 from Mirah. In response, Himley played havoc with the bowling, running up 157 for the loss only of six wickets. Towards the Day (47), Stevens (39), and Bird (34 not out) played splendid cricket for their runs. This is quite the best effort of Himley this season.
Old Hill Mutual have had a successful time during the holidays. On Saturday they played a very even draw with Netherton, Old Hill making 108, and Netherton 91 for eight wickets. E. H. Wright took five wickets for 15 runs, and batted in capital style for 23, F. Hill also showing good form for his 23. Hotchkiss, though giving one or two chances, showed by far the best form for Netherton, and deserves a special work for his big innings of 51. On Monday the Mutuals won by four wickets and 17 runs against Cradley Heath. Thomings and Hill for the Mutuals, and Butler for the Heathens, showed up with the bat. T. Wright could not do wrong with the ball, and had the remarkable analysis of 4 for no runs.
