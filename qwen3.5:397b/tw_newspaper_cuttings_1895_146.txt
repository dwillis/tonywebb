SWINDERBY AND COLLINGHAM v. LINCOLN v LINDUM (A TEAM).—These teams met at Swinderby on Saturday, in stormy weather, neither side being fully represented. Strawson having lost the toss, the Lindum had to take the field. The home team were first represented by Wigram and Loveday, the bowling being shared by the Lindum captain and Fellowes. With the score at nine Wigram was bowled, and Loveday also lost his wicket three runs later. F. K. Long and Ulyton left at 18, all having been bowled by Fellowes, who had taken four wickets for five runs at this period of the game. Two runs later White was bowled by Strawson, and Broadbent, who had followed in, returned the next ball to the bowler, six wickets being down for seventeen. Upon the Rev. Long and Staley becoming associated runs came more freely, and it was not until forty had appeared on the telegraph board that a separation could be effected. Staley, who had made 13, being the first to leave. At 43 Millins was caught at the wicket, while four runs later Easton was run out. When the score had reached 50 Kennedy was caught at short slip, and the innings closed, Fellowes having taken seven wickets for 26, and Strawson two for 20. Tweed and S. T. Watkins were opposed by Wigram and F. K. Long, and with the fourth ball of the former's first over Watkins was smartly captured in the slips by White. Weldon filled the vacancy, and runs came faster. At thirteen Blyton went on to bowl in place of Long, and at 21 Weldon was caught by White off Wigram. Fellowes then joined Tweed, and the score continued to rise. At thirty-eight White was given a trial with the ball, the change working well, for at 44 Tweed sent one from the new bowler into E. K. Long's hands, and one run later Fellowes returned one to Wigram. H. G. Watkins left at 48, and Cousins was joined by his captain, who, after making a couple of singles, left it with Cousins to make the winning hits, but the latter lost his wicket in the same over, Strawson leaving at the same total. Jeffery left at 56, and after running a bye Charles was run out, the innings closing for 67. The home team went in a second time and had made 24 for three wickets when time was called. All three wickets fell at ten to Strawson, who, in trying to catch a sharp return from Staley, had the misfortune to get his thumb knocked up, and had to retire from bowling; his three wickets were obtained for three runs. The Lindum won the match by seven runs. Score:—

LINCOLN STAMP END v. NEWARK TOWN.—The visit of Newark Town to the Stamp End ground is looked upon as the principal home fixture of the season, and as the locals had obtained the assistance of U. H. Daft for Saturday's match exceptional interest attached to the game. Unfortunately for the "gate" the club anticipated, and for the comfort of those who love to witness cricket, the weather was most unfavourable, and it was not until four o'clock that a start could be made. As soon as it was evident there would be a game supporters of the club rapidly appeared on the ground until, considering the weather, there was a good company. The local captain won the toss and decided to bat, sending in Harrod and Mottm to face the bowling of Ringrose and Hollis (pro.). Runs came steadily, but after making five Harrod pt a ball into the hands of Ryan, Kerrigan then partnered Mottam, and a good stand was made, both men playing good cricket. Mottam got several nasty knocks, and was eventually smartly taken at point by Jones. H. H. Daft now partnered Kerrigan, and those present had the pleasure of witnessing quite a treat in the style of batting. Daft began carefully until he obtained complete control over the bowling, and though several changes were made, it was to no effect. Kerrigan and Simpson, to the delight of the locals, also showed their best form, and although they did not score so fast as Daft, they played a confident game, and thoroughly upheld the credit of the club. Whitlock did not stay long, and then Heywood joined Daft to give him an opportunity of making his score fifty, which he was not long in doing, and the innings was closed with the total at 106, Daft being not out 51. Daft's driving was both clean and powerful, several boundary strokes being included in his score, while he showed a complete command of the bat. But for the length of the grass in the out-field the scores of all who made runs would have been larger. The visitors fared but badly on going to the wicket. They had about an hour to play, with a chance of being defeated, while at the best they could only expect to make a draw. With six wickets down for 23, rails came on and prevented further play. Richardson smartly threw out Dyer, and a good throw in by the same player enabled Brown to run out Dr. Ringrose; a skilful catch at the wicket by Brown getting rid of Dyer. Of the others Heywood bowled Job, Kerrigan caught and bowled Brown, and Cherry was out his wicket off Harrod. Altogether the locals are to be congratulated on the game they played. Score:—

SKEGNESS AND DISTRICT v. NOTTS FORESTERS.—The opening match of the season came off on Whit Monday. The weather in the morning was squally and threatening, but luckily play was not interfered with during the day. Skegness winning the toss, W. Kitwood and the Skegness professional (Dench) opened for the home eleven, and some good cricket was shown by the batsmen on a rather tricky wicket, Kitwood being first to leave after making 13 runs, Dench playing on shortly after to Morley for 14. E. N. Ashton was at the wickets some time before he was c and b to Hall for a steadily played innings of 10 runs. T. Brearey going in sixth wicket down, with the score standing at 17, made matters rather lively, contributing five fours to the boundary before he was disposed of a hard-hit 31 runs, and the total carried to 80. No further stand was made, the venture closing for 95 runs. The visitors, after the luncheon interval, sent in J. Mackey and M. P. Smith to face the bowling of Dr. Cross and Dench. Twenty-one runs were registered before Mackey was sent back to a splendid catch low down at mid-off by Kitwood, the second wicket also falling to another grand catch by Kitwood—two for 36. A. T. Oliver and M. F. Smith getting set runs were made at a good pace. Dench was here relieved by A. M. Wilson, and 78 runs had been made when Oliver was stumped by Newton off Cross for a well-played 23—five for 78. The game now looked in favour of the visiting team who only wanted 18 runs with five wickets to fall. However, so well did Dr. Cross bowl, the remaining five batsmen put on only eight runs, all falling to the Doctor, who certainly must be congratulated on his maiden venture with the leather for the Skegness Club, his bowling analysis reading 10 overs, 4 maidens, 34 runs, 11 wickets, the home team winning their opening match by the narrow majority of nine runs. SKEGNESS AND DISTRICT v. COOPER COBAN (LEICESTER).—The second match of the season was played on Tuesday last, and was chiefly remarkable for another fine bowling performance by Dr. Cross, who went even "one better" than on the previous day when playing Notts Foresters, as against the visitors from Leicester he took nine wickets at a cost of only ten runs, the home team winning easily owing to the fine display of bowling already referred to. For Skegness W. Kitwood and Dr. Cross opened against the deliveries of Eastwood and Porring, W. Kitwood, who was in good form, doing most of the run-getting. T. Wilson made some good hits for his ten runs, and E. N. Ashton played steady cricket for a like number. Dench and Kitwood played good cricket while together, the latter being eventually bowled by Eastwood for a well-played innings of 38 runs. The professional's wicket was the last to fall, after making 24 in good form, the innings closing for 113 runs. The team from Leicester sent in Towers and Mountney to the bowling of Dr. Cross and Dench. With the second ball delivery Epton caught Towers at mid-off, off Cross, and five wickets were actually down to Cross in the first four overs delivered for three runs. The only one to make any stand was Mountney, who made 18 not out by steady cricket, the Leicester team being all out for the meagre score of 41 runs. Dr. Cross's analysis is again worthy of mention, viz.—Ten overs, four balls, five maidens, ten runs, nine wickets. Score:—
