ST. ALBANS v. J. W. DUJARDIN LEONARD'S XI.
This match was played on the Clarence Ground, St. Albans, on Saturday, when G. H. Small assumed the duties of captain for the first time, in succession to the Hon. W. Grimston, who had resigned for private reasons. The visitors were not so strong as it was anticipated they would be, and they also came one short, but a very pleasantly contested game resulted from the meeting of the teams, the outcome being a victory for the home side, who are now playing very strongly. Winning the toss, the visitors first handled the willow, but the start was a very bad one, F. S. Young playing the first ball delivered on his pads whence it rebounded to the wicket. Five runs only had been added when K. Farlow was caught in the slips by Margetson of Sansom, while with the total at seven Bolt was bowled by Pearce, and at nine Day held an easy catch in the slips off Sansom which caused the retirement of Dujardin Leonard. A. Farlow soon began to hit, and the score was carried to 21 ere another wicket fell, and six and seven runs respectively were added for the sixth and seventh wickets. Farlow was bowled at the same total by Pearce, who obtained another wicket in the same over, and Clabon being absent, the innings was brought to a close.
G. H. Small and Field opened the venture for St. Albans, and at the luncheon interval had scored 35 without loss. On resuming 14 runs were speedily added, and Field was then bowled in trying to pull a slow ball from Farlow to leg. Westell, who followed in, hit with considerable freedom, but having scored 24 he was sent back by Hooper, while two runs late Day was smartly caught by Morgan off Farlow at deep mid-off. The century was hoisted with Small and Sansom together, but the former was then splendidly caught by Morgan at deep mid-off off Farlow, and Pearce was got rid of in a similar manner. Simmons was bowled after scoring three, but Sansom and Foxley made a stand, carrying the score from 123 to 162, at which total the former was bowled by Morgan. Spencer batted carefully for 11, the second century being hoisted before he retired, and at 224, Foxley, who had showed very good form, was out lbw to Farlow, the retiring batsman having made 53, the highest score of the day, by good cricket. Margetson soon became busy, but was caught after making 10, and the innings closed for 210. Dickenson being not out 9. There were 43 extras, ten of which were no-balls.
On batting a second time the visitors made a better show, but six wickets were down for 56 runs when stumps were drawn. Score and analysis :—

ST. ALBANS "A" v. PINCHLEY 2ND.
The St. Albans "A" team journeyed to Finchley with a weak team, and the outlook was not at all a favourable one, as on the occasion of their visit to St. Albans, the home side was defeated. Cricket, however, is a game of surprises, and one was certainly furnished on this occasion, for the Albanians won very comfortably, thanks principally to the destructive bowling of C. Taylor, and the excellent batting of Taylor and H. C. Webdale. The home side won the toss, and opened their venture with Grear and Harris opposed to the bowling of Taylor and H. C. Webdale. The trundlers soon proved they were in good form, the latter, who went on with lobs, getting a wicket in his first over, while Taylor got one in his second, following this up by obtaining another wicket when the score had reached ten. When C. Dalgleish and Hawkins became associated a better aspect was put on the game, and aided by some loose fielding they added 32 runs before the lob bowler beat the former, and at the same total Taylor bowled Hawkins. Taylor then carried all before him, bowling with such deadly effect that he sent up five overs for five wickets and four runs, the innings closing for 54. The Saints made a bad start, two wickets being down for 18 runs, but Taylor and Webdale then got together, and defying the efforts of the bowlers, they remained together until 60 appeared on the board, and the match was won. Wickets then came down more rapidly, but the total realised was 88. Grear and Hawkins were the most effective bowlers for the home side, and for St. Albans Taylor took seven wickets for 23 runs. ST. ALBANS "B" TEAM v. ST. ALBANS GRAMMAR SCHOOL.
This match was played on the Grammar School ground on Saturday, victory resting with the Club, who sent too strong a team for the school. Batting first the Grammar School made only 37, but this was partly due to the bad judgment shown in running, three men losing their wickets from this cause. Hartley and F. Dudley bowled with deadly effect, the former securing four wickets for 15 runs, and the latter three for eleven. When the Albanians went in to bat, Hartley was missed from the first ball he received, and this mistake proved very expensive, as he then hit away merrily, the first wicket falling at 66. Parry, E. J. Mitchell, Dr. Fallon, H. Mayer, and J. W. Sharpe all reached double figures, and the total realised was 152. Scores :—

ST. ALBANS "A" TEAM v. MR. FOLD'S XI.
This full day match was played on the Clarence Recreation Ground, on Wednesday, in delightful weather. Mr. Folds, who lives at North Mimms, and got together a strong team, and winning the toss the visitors had first innings. Four runs only had been scored when H. C. Webdale who went on with lobs, bowled Wimbury, but with Bamford and T. Pearce together runs came freely, 48 being totalled ere the former was stumped by Simmons. C. Westell remained while over 20 were added, 81 being up when three wickets were down, but after Pearce was got rid of for 46, wickets fell fast before the attack of Barker and Margetson, the former taking four for 30, and the latter four for 11, the innings closing for 106. After luncheon interval the home side opened their venture, but a most disastrous start was made. Day, Bert Webdale and H. C. Webdale were out when the total had reached five, Barker was run out when the score at 17, and at 29, Taylor, who had hit up 21 in good style was caught and bowled by Pearce, who one run later got rid of Parry in the same way—six for 30. The game now appeared to be over, but on Harvey joining Margetson a determined resistance was offered to the bowling, and 41 runs had been added for that wicket when the latter retired for a doubtful lbw, having made 29 runs—an invaluable innings. The most extraordinary part of the cricket was to come. T. H. Wilkie part-oral Harvey, the runs came freely, and a century quickly appeared. Ten after ten were added, the ball being sent to all parts of the field, and the score reached 177, or 106 for that wicket, when Wilkie was bowled by Pearce after making 49, included in his innings being some fine drives and leg hits. Harvey was bowled by Pearce at 198, having compiled 67, and the value of his contributions cannot be over-estimated. He was in with the score at 30 for six wickets, when a severe defeat seemed inevitable, and remained while 168 runs were added. The innings closed for 192, Pearce taking seven wickets for 49 runs. On batting a second time the visitors lost six wickets for 69 runs. Score :—
