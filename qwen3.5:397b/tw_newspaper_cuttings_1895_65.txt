WEST BRITON : THURSDAY 22 AUGUST 1895 - 2.

THE OLD PROBIANS' CRICKET TOUR.
It was a weather tour more than a cricket tour. If we had had as much cricket as weather, we should have had what a genial friend of mine calls a "hurrah" time. But I must not say all I feel about the weather we had, because the West Briton has a very wide circulation.
Matches had been arranged with Porthcurnow (two days), Redruth, Camborne, and Old Mannamedians (two days). Of these we played the first two only, winning at Porthcurnow and losing at Redruth.
We were not so strong as in '93 and '94, for we had to do without such a brilliant trio as W. H. Trewceke, R. Jackson, and Arthur Teague, our three best men. The first-named was holiday-making at Ilfracombe, the second could not leave his work, and the third is, sad to say, in Africa. By the way, he is not on his way home. What a help he would have been to the O.O.C.C. this year!
However, a goodly little band was got together, formed of F. T. Handsombody, E. Howell, W. Tresawna, E. Pascoe, H. Pascoe (the skipper), R. Maunsell, J. K. Blackmore, S. Lawry, H. Spratt, H. E. Handsombody, E. Bullen, L. Dale, J. Teague, G. E. Trewceke, E. Ash, and H. E. Reynolds.
As usual, we put up at the Western at Penzance, and were most comfortably cared for there. It's the best hotel we ever go to. At 11 on the Monday we drove out to Porthcurnow in a "sharrybang," and as we did not stop to see St. Buryan Church this time, we arrived in good time. After lunch we set to work to decide the tennis contest - for I must tell you that we had arranged four trials of skill with the Exiles, viz., in cricket, tennis, billiards, and swimming. Our representatives were F. Handsombody and E. Pascoe, H. Spratt and E. Ash, G. Trewceke and E. Bullen. The Exiles relied on Spencer and Biron, Taylor and Withers, Rowe and a man I must call A. N. Other, as I have forgotten his name. The games were played on two splendid asphalt courts, and the result was a draw slightly in favour of the Exiles, who were one game ahead in a third and deciding set, when rain came on. I give a fuller score below, and will only add here that the best players were Spencer, Biron, Rowe, Handsombody, E. Ash, and Pascoe. After an excellent dinner, we took to billiards. In the doubles - 250 up - the players were Booth and Withers (Porthcurnow) v. Dale and Reynolds (Probians). We won an interesting game by 47, the best break being a 30 by Reynolds.
Dale easily won the singles for us, scoring 250 to 164 by Withers. The winner displayed good and consistent form all through the game. To add to our happiness, there was a dance in the Theatre, and as many charming girls were there, the skipper and one or two others were quite in their element. But having to play cricket on the next day, our captain took us all off at eleven, and after a wet drive we reached the western at half-past twelve, too tired for supper, but not for other refreshment.
Next morning the skipper, who has a dreadful habit of waking very early, called us all at seven, and took us down to the swimming bath, and there he and "Tommy" gave us a capital exhibition of high diving. The rest of us preferred a less graceful way of entering the water. We had a delightful drive out to Porthcurnow - two or three, as usual, being taken in over the Buryan Tower Trick - and a cordial welcome when we got there. We lost the toss, but were sent in, and there we stayed till we got 163, to which Blackwell contributed a steady 40 and Tresawna a stylish 10 (not out), the latter and Howell putting on 27 for the last wicket. Biron got six of our wickets for 31 - a very good performance. On the Exiles going in Bullen and Handsombody bowled so well that they were all out for 31, of which Spencer got a lucky 17. Bullen's analysis showed five for five. This speaks for itself. In the follow-on, owing to some brilliant and clean hitting by Davies, they got 63 for two wickets. Between the innings Mrs. Ash and other ladies very kindly gave us tea. That same evening we had a right royal time. We dined with Mr. and Mrs. Ash, and afterwards witnessed a dress rehearsal of "The Cantab" - a farce in which Mr. Booth showed eminent histrionic power. He was very ably supported by the Misses Thorne and Ash, and by Messrs. Winfield and Fisher. The ladies looked extremely pretty, too. A smoking concert followed, and here again Mr. Booth displayed his versatile abilities in some excellently sung comic songs. Messrs. Rainer, Skee, Thorne, Bullen, and E. Pascoe also added to the musical portion of the programme, and Mr. Peel gave a clever exposition of club swinging. It was nearly midnight when we left for Penzance amid cheers and counter-cheers, vows of good fellowship and friendship, and promises of return. We were all truly sorry to go, for a dearer, sweeter little place than Porthcurnow, and a better set of sportsmen than the Exiles can't be found anywhere. I myself would like to play them a six-days' match. It was a perfect night, and the drive to Penzance was so lovely that even the most unmusical of us sang of "Honey, My Honey," &c., and I must except the skipper - he had gone down the Ohio with a personal friend.
We got to Redruth the next day soon after eleven, and got quarters at Tabbs, where we were delighted to find our president and his daughter. Rain delayed the game till three, and then having lost the toss again we had to bowl and field under a disadvantage. We had them down for 65, but made a sorry show ourselves against the really good bowling of Letcher, who got seven wickets for eight runs. Gray, too, kept wicket admirably, standing right up to Letcher, who was getting a fair amount of piece on. On Redruth going in again they had a merry time, and had made 83 for half their wickets, when we had to stop. Rule played good cricket for his runs, but the others were decidedly lucky, and were at times completely stuck up with slow dropping full tosses. We deserved to lose though, as our fielding - bar a grand catch by Blackmore at point - was very indifferent, and we batted with little nerve against Letcher.
In the evening we went to see the Pantomime Secretary, and laughed heartily as dear old Cattermole, who was simply immense - bien entendu.
At Camborne next day no start was possible, as it rained hard all the afternoon. All we could do was to eat lunch, play billiards, and then have tea. To make matters worse for us, a telegram came during the afternoon cancelling the Old Mannamedians match, because no ground could be secured to play on. A Cabinet Council was hurriedly called, and it was decided to try and get a match on with Penzance; but the Penzance men could not play. Then some daring spirit suggested a trip to Scilly. Thereupon some star-eyed youth - I think it was the skipper - told us a sickening story of the sea, of which said story the chief characters were Daisy, her grandpapa, and some currants. So Scilly was promptly off. Then our scientist had a brilliant idea. It was this. To form ourselves into an antiquarian society and do Dartmoor. It was carried nem.con., and three of us were deputed to go to Plymouth that very night and make arrangements. So we went, and travelled up part of the way with that good and enthusiastic cricketer, the rector of Camelford, and he told us many good stories. We hope to arrange a match with him next year. Well, we made all arrangements, but to no purpose, for the others never came on account of the wretched weather. So we three were left to enjoy ourselves as best we could, and with the help and hospitality of our good friend H. James, we did very well indeed. We had very comfortable quarters at the Lockyer, and we spent a most enjoyable evening with Miss Dorothy Bantam, the Lurcher, and all others of the "Dorothy" company. For this we were indebted to our good friend mentioned above. We poured it all day Saturday, so that even if we had got a match with the Old Mannamedians we couldn't have played. And so our third tour came to an inglorious end. I have not made out any average for obvious reasons. I merely append results.
