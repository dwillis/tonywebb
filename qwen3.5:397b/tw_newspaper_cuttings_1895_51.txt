Stockport v. Little Lever.
This match was played at Little Lever in splendid weather and before a large attendance of spectators. A start was not made till quite three o'clock owing to the Stockport men being delayed on the road. The match was set apart for the benefit of Perry, one of the Little Lever professionals, and this being his first year with them, Stockport still deplored the services of Fred Torkington and one or two others, while Little Lever had their strongest team in the field this season. Little Lever won the toss, and electing to bat, sent in Towler and Tyldesley to the bowling of Brown and F. Tyler. Tyldesley had a splendid reception on coming out—a fact due, no doubt, to that player being admitted to the Lancashire County ranks, and it will be remembered that on his second appearance for his county he scored no fewer than 153 not out against Warwickshire. Brown opened to Towler, beating him first ball, and the next ball bowled him leg stump. A. Smith came next, but after scoring six in a wretched fashion Brown upset his wicket. G. Smith, the Lancashire County wicket-keeper, joined Tyldesley, but immediately after Tyldesley was splendidly taken by Bates right on the boundary, and three good wickets were down for seven. Peel came next, and soon began to force matters, hitting Brown out of the ground for four. We may say the ground is a very small one indeed, it being no trouble to get a ball out. Smith seemed content with singles. The score rose steadily, and a change of bowling was tried at 30, Harry May displacing Tyler; the change almost worked immediately, May bowling Peel for a well hit 20. Hallows came next, and got May away to leg nicely for three, but at the other end Brown bowled him with a grand ball, and half the wickets were down for 45 runs. Simm joined Smith; the latter with his score at 16 was missed by Bates at long off. Simm was the next to go, Brown upsetting his wicket. 51—6—0. Knutton followed, and hit up 11 in four hits, but was then beautifully taken by Harold Hartley off Brown. 55—7—11. Northrop came next, and bore a little stand was made, Northrop getting May away for three while Smith cut the same bowler for a like number. The score had reached 81 when Brown disturbed Smith's wicket for a lucky innings of 32. A strange coincidence took place during Smith's innings, and one which certainly did not reflect much credit on the Little Lever umpire. W. Hartley, who had displaced May, was bowling his last ball of his second over, Smith hit the ball out to Bates at square leg and was caught, the umpire, in the meantime, saying "over," and when Bates had caught Smith the umpire gave it a "no ball," and thus Smith was allowed to proceed with his innings. The end soon came, the innings closing for 91. Sam Brown deserves great credit for his splendid bowling, taking no fewer than eight wickets for 37 runs, hitting the stumps six times, while the other two were caught. Stockport opened their venture in a dreadful bad light, J. Bates and W. Hartley being the first batsmen to face Knutton and Penny. Bates after scoring a single was caught at the wicket. A Torkington joined his captain, but after scoring five shared the fate of Bates. Brown came next, and hitting one out to Tyldesley was splendidly caught, and three wickets were down for 11 runs. Rain then commenced to fall, and put an end to all further play, and what would have been an interesting finish was spoiled. The Stockport team folded quite up to form. On Saturday next Worsley are down at Cale Green, and this match is set apart for the benefit of Sam Brown and Jim Dainty. As this is one of the best fixtures on the card and a fixture which takes well with the Stockport people, the two "pros." should materially benefit by it. Given a fine day a grand game is expected.
