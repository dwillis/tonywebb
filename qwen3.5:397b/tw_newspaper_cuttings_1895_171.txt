The excellent all-round display of the Norfolk team in the opening day's play at Cambridge met with its reward on Thursday, when the Cambridgeshire eleven were defeated by the handsome margin of 6 wickets. Less than two hours sufficed to bring the match to an end on the second day, and the players were in Norwich, receiving the congratulations of their friends, by six in the evening. Shore again bowled finely, capturing 6 wickets for 55 runs, which brought his record for the match to eleven for 90. Morley only secured two for 33 in the second innings, but his average for the match (seven for 54) is a trifle better than the left hander's. The credit of the victory is of course largely due to the excellent trundling of these two men. They were backed up by smart fielding; and it is worthy of note that F. A. Fryer, who stumped three men, only allowed a single bye in each innings. In Norfolk's first innings there were no fewer than 21 byes.

Can any reader call to mind a season in which Oxfordshire figured in the Norfolk programme? I am inclined to the opinion that the matches were arranged for the first time this season. As readers are doubtless aware, this extension of the programme was brought about by the desire to qualify for the second-class counties' championship, which is open to any team able to arrange four home and home county matches among the counties assenting to the scheme. There would appear to be some doubt as regards Norfolk's position in the present contest. Although a score or more counties approved of the championship only six became qualified to take part in it. The six were Durham, Norfolk, Staffordshire, Oxfordshire, Bedfordshire, and Worcestershire. The last-named arranged the heaviest programme, and at one time it was a neck-and-neck race between that county and Durham for first place. Last week the two met at Kidderminster, and after a good match the Worcestershire men secured the verdict by 43 runs. According to a contemporary, this victory placed Worcestershire at the head of affairs. It was stated that five victories had been achieved against two defeats, giving a margin of three points. This is precisely the same number of points Norfolk has obtained. Perhaps Mr. Foley will oblige with the official figures.

It is seldom a team appears on both University grounds in one and the same week. Mr. Burton's men did this last week, when Oxfordshire and Cambridgeshire were met. The return match with Oxfordshire was originally fixed to take place at Wisley, but at the last moment the Oxfordshire Executive were compelled to make an alteration, and the venue was changed to Oxford city.

It was a new experience for several members of the team, and a lot of sight-seeing was indulged in. An accident happened during the first evening of their visit which might have been attended with serious results. Several of the team were assisting at a circus entertainment when the promenade suddenly gave way, and a number of persons were precipitated unceremoniously to the "behind the scenes" part of the establishment. Fortunately no one was seriously hurt, though one Norfolcian complained of the shock to his nervous system.

The Biarritz correspondent of the New York Herald in describing a cricket match played in the town under the title of England and America v. Spain, says "It resulted in a victory for the Anglo-Saxons, Mr. Watkyn and Mr. Delas hitting the bowling of the Dons to such an extent under a burning sun that the field gradually melted away, and, the batsmen finding themselves alone, decided to declare the innings closed and adjourn to the villa, where they found the truant eleven playing poker, and partaking of things iced."

DR. THOMSON'S ELEVEN v. H. COZENS-HARDY'S ELEVEN.
This game was played in delightful weather at Thorpe Asylum on Saturday. The pretty ground was in capital condition, though the pitch, thanks to the hot sunshine of the past few days, now and then kicked awkwardly. The visitors were most hospitably entertained, and after a thoroughly interesting match were defeated by eight wickets. They won the toss, and batting first were all disposed of for 62, Fiddy and Lieutenant Searight carrying all before them. Horne and Marshall alone reached double figures. Dr. Thomson's side passed their opponents' total with seven wickets down, the innings eventually reaching 94. Cowles being the chief scorer with 23. The visiting team made a better show in their second venture. Everett, Smith, and Marshall played free cricket, and were mainly responsible for raising the score to 118 for five wickets, when the innings was declared closed, and Dr. Thomson's eleven were left with 85 to win and an hour and five minutes to make them. Cowles was again in form, and, with Wilson and Captain Matthews, knocked off the required runs in three-quarters of an hour, the task being rendered easier by the absence of Heslop, who bowled excellently in the first innings, but who was obliged to leave before five o'clock.
