BIRKENHEAD NEWS : SATURDAY 14 SEPTEMBER 1895 - 2.

CRICKET NOTES.

Saturday saw the practical conclusion of the season, the leading local clubs all finishing their list of engagements. The Premier club should have played Huyton, but as the latter's ground was nothing short of a quagmire after the heavy rain of the previous evening, the match had perforce to be abandoned. It is some time back now since the Parkites have had such a successful season as the one just concluded, as their position at the head of the district competition readily testifies. Independent, however, of their excellent record against the district clubs, they have done more than well against such powerful opponents as those of Manchester, Leyland and Wigan, the latter being beaten in both their engagements with the Park club. It will be seen on reference to the batting and bowling averages that T. R. Hubback heads the list in both departments of the game, a position which indicates what an invaluable acquisition he has been to his club with both bat and ball, independent of the great service he has rendered behind the wicket. R. Fielding Ould has secured the second position in the batting list, being closely followed by H. J. Fish, who, owing to a severe illness has been prevented from taking part in several important matches. On the whole skipper Holden can hardly be said to have played up to the form exhibited by him for several previous years, and it looks a trifle curious to see his name so low down in the list as fourth, but still an average of 31.11 for 26 innings is by no means bad, and besides this he has rendered excellent service with the ball, having captured 45 wickets at an average cost of 9.20. Indeed he has seldom bowled better than he has done this season, and he is to be congratulated on his capital record with the sphere. H. M. Grayson comes out fairly well with an average of 19.1 for 16 innings, but Smith shows a great falling off as compared with last year, his figures being 16.3 for 19 ventures. Town has done a little better, he having an average of 18 for 13 innings, whilst others who figure rather low down, but who may be confidently expected to do much better in the future, include H. W. P. Bennette, H. Salmon, and H. S. Cochrane. That good old stonewaller, A. B. Wilson, does not appear to have exercised his usual discretion, for he comes very near to being the "absolute," this unenviable position being occupied by Captain Byrne. The brunt of the bowling has of course being borne by Smith, who has taken 88 wickets for 10.9, whilst O. E. Steen's 48 have been secured at a rate of 13.7. Town has had a singularly unfortunate season, as although he has bowled uniformly well he has been able to get but few wickets, 24 at a cost of 22.11 being rather expensive. The Rock Ferryites concluded a fairly successful season on Saturday with a win over Cheadle Hulme. Of the 25 matches they have played, 11 have been won, 6 drawn, and 7 lost, whilst one had the almost unique result of a tie. A glance at the batting averages shows E. F. Kearsey at the head of affairs with the nice figures of 30.88 for nine innings, and it seems rather a pity that his services are not more often available for first team matches. A. J. Cripps comes next, but his average compares unfavourably with that of Kearsey, there being a considerable difference between 30.88 and 23, albeit Cripps has played two more innings. With the exception of his captain, A. Stott, who is placed third, has taken part in more matches than any other player in the team, and his 21.13 for 24 innings must be set down as distinctly creditable. G. G. Paton has an average of 20.4 for 14 innings, whilst immediately following him comes "Capting" Ravenscroft who, on the whole, has been far from fortunate, repeated failures reducing his figures to as low as 19.79 for 26 innings. Another batsman who has not shown to great advantage this season is T. R. Bretherton, whose figures of 18.78 for 20 attempts are not as good as might be expected from such a capable willow wielder, whilst the average of A. C. Blain is also a little disappointing, this usually remarkably vigorous batsman being comparatively low down in the list with 18.33 for 18 innings. Needham's figures are 17.28 for 23 attempts, the next in order of merit being A. C. Bamford, who for 16 innings has an average of 14.46. Surprisingly low down is H. S. Bamford who, however, has not devoted much time to the game, having only been at the wickets on six occasions, for which he has an average of 10.14. J. Bretherton, E. Stott, G. B. Paterson, and E. C Rylands are also allotted places in the list, the last being Ryland who, out of 14 innings, has been not out no less than seven times, and would probably have had a much higher average than 9.14 if he had been given a chance of showing his abilities with the bat. If he has not proved himself a brilliant batsman, however, there can be no question about his success with the ball, for during the season he has captured no fewer than 113 wickets at the moderate rate of 11.03 per wicket. J. Bretherton, though bowling well, falls far behind Kyland, the fast bowler only crediting himself with 83 wickets at a cost of 12.36. With such strong amateur bowling talent at its disposal, the club was not required to call upon the services of Needham to any great extent, but the pro has been very useful at times, and has secured 26 wickets at an average cost of 17.19. Though the Oxtonites have been under a cloud for the last two or three weeks, they cannot be said to have fared badly on the whole, as out of 28 matches played they have won 9 and drawn 13, being absolutely beaten on but five occasions, one, the match referred to in my remarks about Rock Ferry, resulting in a tie. On Saturday, at Aigburth, they met with another serious reverse, receiving a shocking trouncing at the hands of the Liverpool representatives, who disposed of them for 71, and knocked up 133 for the loss of seven wickets. The Oxtonites thus ended the season with three successive losses. Their batting averages reveal the fact that M. Court has not only scored the most runs, but he has also the best average, his figures being 32.67 for 27 innings, a really excellent record for this very promising young player. The second position on the list is filled by J. H. P. Grayson, with 27.87 for nine innings, but now that this undoubtedly fine batsman has settled down and definitely thrown in his lot with the Oxtonites, we may expect to see a marked improvement in his figures next year. Another old Park player takes third honours, this being W. Turner, who has developed into one of the mainstays of the Oxton batting, this being evident by his excellent average of 26.67 for 25 innings. The Oxton skipper, C. F. Hutton has been a little off colour occasionally during the season, and has not scored quite so prolifically as he has done in former years, his highest contribution in an innings being 52, and his average 20.38 for 15 ventures. That the new pro. Bishop will be a great acquisition to the Oxtonites next season there can be but little doubt, for during the short time he has been engaged he has done wonderfully well with both bat and ball, having a batting average of 19.29 for 9 innings, whilst with the ball he has secured 48 wickets at a cost of 9.4 per wicket. J. Curwen has scored better and more consistently than he has done for a considerable time, and his record of 19.12 for 20 innings indicates that his powers as a batsman have not yet altogether left him. H. T. Hibbert has only taken part in 12 matches, for which his figures are 16.17, whilst for 19 innings M. Field has an average of 14.22. Through an injury received early on in the season, Smith was unable to take as active a part in the campaign as could be wished, and for his 12 innings he is rather low down on the list with 13.52. H. Bickersteth has played in ten matches, and has scarcely been so successful in taking out his bat as he has been in former seasons, consequently his average has suffered somewhat, and his figures of 10.85 are not calculated to make him unduly conceited. G. H. Court, B. S. Smith, and P. L. Bromnell are all close together with figures ranging from 10.33 to 8.18, L. Court bringing up the rear with 3.86 for 2 innings. Bowling honours are divided between amateurs and professionals, Bishop coming our well with 41 wickets at a cost of 9.4. Smith has proved a trifle more expensive his figures being 63 for 9.62, but his record is an excellent one for all that, more especially in view of the accident he met with, before referred to. G. H. Court has also rendered splendid service with the ball, capturing no less than 49 wickets, whilst his younger brother L. Court has given signs of future usefulness by securing 12 wickets for 3.86 per wicket. Like that of Oxton, the Birkenhead Victoria first string have been faring badly of late, and also like Oxton they concluded their campaign on Saturday with a disastrous defeat, the Bootle representatives, who have done anything but well themselves this year, achieving an easy victory over the evidently disorganised Victorites, who made a very feeble show with the bat, and were all sent to the rightabout for 71. On the whole the Victoria representatives can hardly be congratulated on their season's record, for out of 25 matches played they have only won four, whilst 9 have been lost and 12 drawn. It is not surprising to see J. Wylde at the head of the batting averages with 25.14, for he has shown consistent good form throughout the season. Others who may be said to have done well are W. T. Davies (22.14), F. Wright (19.4) W. H. Routledge (18.7) and J. Macdonald (17.3), but a falling off is noticeable in the batting of J. Brooklebank, who is very low down on the list with 14.6, though he has played in more matches than any of his colleagues. J. Fish has been a distinct acquisition with the leather and heads the bowling averages with 27 wickets for a trifle over 11 runs per wicket. So far as taking wickets is concerned, however, J. Brooklebank is an easy first, he having captured 42 at a cost of 14.32. Several others of the team have had a trial with the ball, these including J. West, S. Wright, M. Sprat, J. Wylde, E. H. Carter, and W. H. Routledge.
