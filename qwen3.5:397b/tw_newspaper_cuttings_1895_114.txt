ST. ALBANS v. WEST MIDDLESEX.

On Friday St. Albans journeyed to Acton to play West Middlesex in the latter's cricket week, but unfortunately the visitors were unable to raise anything like a representative team, as beside the county match at the Oval some of the best men were unable to play. Batting first, the home side sent G. W. Beldam and A. W. Malden to the wickets, and these two gave a fine exhibition of batting, the former particularly playing a splendid game. All the bowlers were played with the greatest ease, and it was not until the score had reached 171 that the first wicket was obtained, Westell then clean bowling Malden, who had placed 62 to his credit. The luncheon interval arrived with the score at 188 for one wicket, and on resuming, runs still came freely, but at 234 a second wicket was secured, and shortly afterwards Beldam's long and correctly played innings came to a close, his individual total being 121. The ground, which is small and surrounded by trees, was now very bad for the batsmen, as heavy clouds had loomed up, and five other wickets fell for the addition of 26 runs, the innings being declared closed at 259 for seven wickets. When St. Albans went in to bat their chances were not bright, the light was very bad, and Beldam bowled at such a pace that it was almost impossible to follow the ball, the result being that the batting collapsed, and the smallest score of the season was made. Pearce and Westell were alone able to make a stand, and the side was disposed of for 45 runs, eight of the wickets being captured by Beldam for 27 runs—a very fine performance. On batting a second time the visitors did much better, but this may be accounted for by the fact that the fast bowler did not trundle. The score and analysis are as follows:—

ST. ALBANS v. DUNSTABLE GRAMMAR SCHOOL.

A most interesting match was played between the above teams on the Clarence Ground on Wednesday, the result being a draw. Dunstable Grammar School have not lost a match for some few years, and as they have been playing so well this season it was anticipated that the home side, which was not a fully representative one, would be beaten. Winning the toss, the visitors, who had a strong side out, starting batting to the bowling of Barker and Field. Twenty-nine runs were quickly scored, and Thring was then caught by H. J. Hill at mid-off off Barker, for a useful 23. Two other wickets fell quickly, but on Manton and Ryder becoming associated, the score was carried to 64, at which total the latter was well caught by Field at deep mid-off from Pearce, the lob bowler. Manton continued to bat in fine form, but could not get anyone to stay with him until Stevenson arrived, and these two advanced the total from 130 to 198, though chances were given which were not accepted. At length Stevenson was smartly caught and bowled by Pearce, Manton was splendidly caught on the boundary off the same bowler by E. R. Hill, who had earlier made a fine catch closer in, and Dr. Norman, also off Pearce, terminated the innings with a brilliant left handed catch at cover point, the total realised being 210. Manton was top scorer with 79, obtained by good cricket. The home side started with Barker and Westell, and both played carefully, as there was no time to make the runs, and it was thought advisable not to run too many risks. With the score at 12, Barker failed to get well hold of a tempting ball from W. F. Brown, and was caught at extra mid-off, but with E. R. Hill and Westell together the score rose rapidly, and a change of bowling was resorted to, Preston displacing Manton. This had the desired effect, E. R. Hill in trying to pull a home toss found to leg being bowled, and another home wicket was put up to Thring by Westell. Pearce was caught at the man at the fine ball he received, but Field and Dr. Norman then remained together until rain stopped play, the score being 77 for four wickets. WEST HERTS v. HEMEL HEMPSTEAD.

As West Herts could not make it convenient to arrange a return fixture last year with the Hemel Hempstead Cricket Club, who in that season visited Watford, Mr. Humbert kindly brought a fairly good team on Saturday last to play at Boxmoor. They did not do themselves justice, however, still thanks to a pluckily hit innings of 45 by L. E. S. Ward, their score reached the very respectable total of 99, which many thought would be a few too many for the homesters. It would have been, no doubt, if West Herts had accepted all the chances offered; as many of the visitors certainly threw away what chance they had of winning by at times very loose fielding, albeit Ward and a few others fielded well. For Hemel Hempstead, Woodman and Badcock put on 32 for the first wicket, when the latter was caught, and Pearce came in, but he left at the same total. Price, Richards, Whitman, and Hall, however, compiled useful scores, and the game was won before the latter left, 7 for 104. The innings finally closed for 112, twenty of which were extras. Hemel Hempstead thus winning by 13 runs. Scores:—

ST. ALBANS v. HOLBORN.

A FIASCO.

The return match between St. Albans and Holborn was fixed to take place on Saturday, the time arranged being two o'clock, and the ground that of the Holborn Club at Crouch End. For the purpose of enabling a start to be made in good time, the visitors left St. Albans by the 12.45 Midland, but they missed their connecting train at Kentish Town and it was half-past two when they reached the locus in quo. To call it a cricket ground would be a misuse of terms, and upon inspecting it some of the visitors were in favour of not making a start at all, as the idea of playing cricket, i.e. —real cricket—was absurd. Another drawback to a start was the fact that at three o'clock only about four of the home side had put in an appearance, but after another half-an-hour's waiting it was agreed to make a commencement, and Holborn went to the wickets. As the afternoon advanced the H. Horn men straggled on to the ground, the last one put in an appearance at five o'clock. When the time arrived for the Albanians to bat some of the most consistent scorers refused to do so, and small blame to them. It was left to the others to do exactly as they pleased, and treating the matter as a huge joke they walked out with their bats with the expressed intention of promptly returning, so as not to run the risk of an injury. Small, who watched the ball very carefully managed to avoid getting hurt, but Westell was more unfortunate, as receiving a nasty blow, the effects of which he will feel for some time to come. When the seventh wicket fell the farce was at an end, and stumps were drawn. Thus ended a most unsatisfactory outing.
