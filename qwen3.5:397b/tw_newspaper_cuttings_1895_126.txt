HERTS ADVERTISER: FRIDAY 16 AUGUST 1895.

V. A. TITCHMARSH.
THE HERTS COUNTY BOWLER.

To all lovers of cricket in this part of the world the name of Titchmarsh is as familiar as a household word. As a county, Herts, it is true, does not rank very high in cricket circles, but most of the triumphs of which it has been able to boast during the past couple of decades have been associated with the name of Titchmarsh, whose bowling, and also batting, have been of the greatest possible service to his side. As a recognition of the valuable services he has rendered to the county of his birth for so long a period, it was unanimously agreed at the last annual meeting that he should have a benefit this year, and the match chosen is the Herts v. Beds, on the County Ground at St. Albans, on August 14th and 15th. That it will be a bumper benefit all good sportsmen heartily wish, for Titchmarsh, after a score of years of hard work, really deserves well of his county, and a poor benefit would be a sorry return for the valuable place he has filled in the Herts cricket team for so long. It was thought that under the circumstances a few details as to Titchmarsh's cricket career would be interesting to our readers, and a representative of the Herts Advertiser accordingly waited upon him a few days since, when a mass of information was placed at his disposal.

The subject of this notice first saw the light at Royston, the date of his advent into this vale of tears being February 14th, 1853, and he is thus now 42 years of age. Some take to cricket as ducks do to water, and young Titchmarsh, who grew into a sturdy boy, early found his chief delight in summer in the King of English games. His excellent batting and bowling as a youngster soon brought him into prominence, and in 1876 he was given a place in the county trial teams. Without doing anything phenomenal he evidently pleased the critics, for in the following year we find him in the county eleven, and the work he did fully justified his inclusion in the team. At times he met with remarkable success in his first season, and he then laid the foundation of a reputation as an able cricketer which he has since so worthily upheld. In those days Herts and Essex regularly met, and there were battles royal between them for supremacy, but Titchmarsh recounts with pardonable pride that he made an exhibition of them at Hitchin, for he secured ten wickets at the ridiculously low cost of 23 runs. That Herts had unearthed a really good bowler was proved as the summer advanced. He seemed to be an object of dread to the Essex batsmen, for in the return at Brentwood he obtained seven wickets for 30 runs, following up this performance by dismissing six Beds batsmen for 15 runs. These are but one or two instances of many unusually good performances, and it was inevitable that news of his ability should spread abroad. His success was not of a meteor-like kind, as during the seasons which followed he quite upheld the high reputation he had already obtained, and in 1879 he was appointed a ground bowler at Oxford University, a post which he retained for five years. Two seasons in succession - the year in which G. F. Grace died and also the preceding one - Titchmarsh accepted invitations to play for the United South of England eleven, and in 1884 he was placed on the ground staff at Lord's, after having gone through a very severe probation. The year previous to that in which he was engaged on the Lord's staff, Titchmarsh played for M.C.C. v. Essex, and was successful both with the bat and the ball. Shortly afterwards he was sent down to Oxford to play for Lord Jersey's XI against Oxford City and County, and came out with flying colours as a bowler, taking 7 wickets for 77 runs against a very strong batting side. In the same week, he played for the M.C.C. against Oxford City and County, and besides some effective trundling he scored 62 runs in fine style. He was all the time steadily progressing in the good opinion of the authorities at Lord's, and when next season came round we find that Titchmarsh was included in the team which went down to Cambridge to play the University, and in this match he obtained five wickets in the first innings and three in the second. He subsequently appeared in the North v. South, and M.C.C. v. Notts matches, and following the latter game he was permanently engaged on the Lord's staff, a post he still occupies.

He has always done well for the M.C.C. both in batting and bowling, having scored several centuries for them, and his good bowling performances are very numerous. In the match in 1886 against Oxford City and County he scored 90 runs, and in 1890 at Blackheath he played 94 runs to his credit, and took five wickets. He was sent down on one occasion to play against Sherborne School, and in the second innings he secured the whole of the ten wickets and scored 31 runs, and he followed this up the next day by going on to Rossall School and scoring 101 not out, at which total the innings was declared closed, while subsequently he obtained six wickets for 50 runs in an innings which realised 163, but the 44 overs he sent down no less than 22 were maidens. In looking through the records which Titchmarsh has preserved, it is impossible to avoid being struck by the fact that batsmen have invariably found him a difficult bowler to score from. He is very deadly, frequently not half-a-dozen balls in twenty overs being off the sticks. This does not mean that he does not bowl with his head. The theory of off bowling is that a bowler should, unless runs are of no consequence, peg away at the stumps, varying pitch, pace, and break so as to entrap the batsman into making unguarded and fatal strokes. Titchmarsh is a bowler with whom liberties must never be taken. Every ball has to be carefully watched, and batsmen must look out for surprises. To return again, after this dissertation, to the actual performances of the subject of this sketch, it should be mentioned that during the M.C.C. Shropshire tour in 1888 he played at Ludlow a fine innings of 54, against Sharpe the Surrey bowler, and other well-known trundlers, and was then run out. His most consistent season for the M.C.C. with bat and ball was perhaps in 1893, when he did some fine work during the Devonshire tour. In the first match, against Exeter, he scored 39 runs, and took eight wickets in the first innings. In the second match versus Torquay he scored 29 for once out, and disposed of eight of his opponents; against Devonshire his total for once out was 70, while against Sidmouth he compiled 43 runs and secured five wickets. Every match of the tour was won, as was also the Oswestry tour in the previous year, in which Titchmarsh took a leading part. In the Northern tour of 1890 Titchmarsh hit up 34 runs at Whitehaven, and accounted for seven wickets; while at Newcastle he was responsible for 32 runs, and captured ten wickets, following this up with 47 runs and 9 wickets at Sunderland, his bowling analysis for the complete tour showing that his wickets had been obtained at the small cost of just over six runs a-piece. Much other excellent work has been done by Titchmarsh, but want of space will not allow us to chronicle in detail much that is worth repeating.

With regard to the county, he has for some years past been the most prominent all-round professional playing for the Club. His fidelity to the county of his birth has not been of much financial advantage to him, as the Herts matches are generally played in the height of the M.C.C. season. It is well, however, that he has remained true to Herts, for without him the county would frequently of recent years have cut a sorry figure. There now seems a likelihood of an improvement, but for seven or eight years previous to last season county cricket in Herts was at a discount. The subscriptions were relatively small, and Mr. C. Pigg, the hon. sec., but for whose efforts the county club would long since have ceased to exist, frequently was unable to put really representative teams in the field. Now and again the side would cover itself with glory, but the batting and bowling were not consistent except in individual cases. In this long period of depression an enormous amount of work fell upon Titchmarsh, and he performed it well. He is capable of bowling hour after hour without getting loose, he is an excellent field, and a tremendously hard worker. Although now over forty years of age, he is in as good form as ever, and he probably never bowled better than last year, when he sent up for the county 607-4 overs, 189 of which were maidens, for 849 runs, and 53 wickets, the average being 11-53. The total number of wickets taken in county matches last year was 106, and Titchmarsh thus obtained half - fine performance. His batting naturally suffers somewhat as a consequence of so much bowling, but his analysis was 13 innings, 222 runs, most in an innings 63, and average 17-77. In 1891, when the county played ten matches, in only one of which they were successful, while five were drawn, Titchmarsh had a batting average of 23-1 for fourteen innings, and he obtained 35 wickets at a cost of just over 15 runs each. In 1892, his batting average was 20-6, and he was a fine form with the ball, taking 38 wickets at an average of 12-26 runs per wicket. In 1893, he was in splendid form again. It is a curious thing that Titchmarsh, although he has during his long cricket career compiled many centuries, has never totalled up a century for his county, though on several occasions he has reached the nineties. In the match v. M.C.C. and Ground at Lord's, in the year under notice, M.C.C. athletes first essay put together 308, Herts replying with 222, of which number Titchmarsh made 32. Having a follow-on the Rev. E. D. Shaw and Titchmarsh started the innings, and both gave a magnificent display, treating the bowling with the utmost contempt, and in two hours they put on 155 runs, when Titchmarsh, who, according to the Sportsman, "played quite of the very best description all through, and was very unfortunate in not making three figures," lost his wicket with his score at 97. Afterwards the Rev. and Hon. E. Lyttelton entered Shaw to raise the score to 270 for one wicket, at which total the innings was declared closed. This meant the match was left drawn, though the county held the winning hand. The batting average of Titchmarsh in 1893 for 17 innings was 23-6, and he obtained 51 wickets at an average cost of a fraction over 15 runs each.
