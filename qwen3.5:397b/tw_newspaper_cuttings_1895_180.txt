ABINGDON HERALD: SATURDAY 29 JUNE 1895 - 2.
THE WALLINGFORD BREWERY v. WORKING MEN'S CLUB.
—The Kine Croft is a pleasant place, but as a cricket field an extension and enlargement at the borough end would be an immense improvement and tend to make it one of the best recreation grounds in the district. It will be remembered a movement in this direction was contemplated in 1887 to commemorate the jubilee year of her Majesty's beneficent reign, but the proposal was nipped in the bud—to the profound disappointment of the public in general and athletes in particular. We yield to no one in praise of St. John's Paddock—the best bit of ground for cricket in Wallingford—which is hired of the Mayor by the W.A.C. at a nominal rent, but we do consider that a well-laid out public recreation ground suitable for all round athletic sports would prove an inestimable boon to present and future generations. With so many public-spirited gentlemen, both within and without the borough, there should be no hesitation in tapping the springs of philanthropy which have never dried up in the cause of charity, and are not destined to do so in the domain of sport while it has liberal friends like Lieut. A. C. Arling, who is an ardent believer in the old adage, mens sana in corpore sano. The Mayor's interest in athletics, too, is proverbial, his Worship being rarely absent from cricket or other contests that come off during the year, and he is ever to the fore in furthering the prospects of outdoor pastimes of which, moreover, he manifests an appreciation most encouraging to the players. The match on Saturday afternoon last, between the Wallingford Brewery and the Wallingford Working Men's Club, was of a very entertaining and eventful character. The Brewery—as brawny a body of men as one would wish to wield the willow—went in first, but ill-luck followed in their wake, the whole side being dismissed for 45 runs, of which number Frewin scored 15 in good style, which included a couple of boundary hits. The Working Men's Club fared slightly worse than their opponents, obtaining only 37, thus losing the game on the first innings by eight runs. The top scorer for the W.M.C. was Hodges, who made a serviceable nine. To J. Norris belongs the honour of making the biggest hit of the day (a 4). The Brewery in their second innings displayed better form, Hinton, Frewin, and J. Sadler, with respective scores of 15, 13, and 11, playing with plenty of vigour and dash. Stumps were drawn at seven o'clock after a four-hours' enjoyable game. The bowling analysis showed some fair results, Hinton taking 3 wickets for 3 runs; Sadler, 4 for 16; C. Sadler, 3 for 16; and H. Hedges 4 for 19. The Umpires were Messrs. Fearce and Groves. Score:

BERKS ASYLUM v. WALLINGFORD ATHLETIC CLUB.
—A cricket match between these two teams came off on Saturday afternoon last at Moulsford, when the W.A.C. gained a substantial victory by 37 runs on the first innings. Arding, Sandall, and Hawkins did yeoman service for the visiting team, the trio making 67 runs between them; whilst for the home team Drs. Murdoch and Johnson and Earnshaw also proved good stayers, they compiling altogether 38. Hatt, as usual, distinguished himself with the ball, he taking no less than seven wickets at a cost of only 37 runs, the best bowling display of the day. Score:

CHERWELL VALLEY v. HOLYWELL.—On Saturday the Holywell A.C. went over to Heyford and met the new Cherwell Valley Club. The latter went in first, and were all got rid of for 65. Holywell commenced their innings very disastrously, losing three wickets for four runs, but the partnership of Craddock and Churns turned the tables. Later on with eleven runs to win and two wickets to fall it was again doubtful whether the required number would be obtained, but Fortescue and Hewlett put on seven and six each, and the score finally reached 69. With three-quarters of an hour more to play the Valley went to the wicket for a second time and scored 35 for the loss of six wickets. Score:
