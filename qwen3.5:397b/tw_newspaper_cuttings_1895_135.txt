WATFORD OBSERVER: SATURDAY 24 AUGUST 1895-3.

WEST HERTS v. ST. ALBANS.
The return match between these teams was played at St. Albans on Wednesday—the second day of the festival. It hardly needs to be repeated on this side of the Colne that the great majority of previous contests both at Watford and St. Albans have resulted in favour of West Herts, and to reduce that majority the city eleven determined to do its best. The game was naturally well balanced, and enthusiasm amongst Albanians ran higher and higher, when it was seen how affairs were going. West Herts lost the toss for the ninth time in succession, and opposed to the bowling of J. Woods and White were Dr. Norman and T. Sansom. Both batsmen reached double figures in safety, but within two runs of 40 the Doctor was bowled by White and Sansom by Woods. G. H. Small, a Beds County man, and A. A. Coles became associated, and it was not long before a change of bowling was considered advisable, R. B. Barker and B. A. F. Astley going on. The alteration had no effect, and the batsmen continued to carry all before them. The century was at last hoisted amidst applause, both batsmen being well set. Nor was a separation effected until the score had been raised to 176, when Small caught Small at third man off Woods's bowling. His score of 87 was made up of six fours, four threes, six twos, and only nine singles. He was given no less than three lives, Little having that number of chances of stumping him, but for all that he was the day's best display. Gilder had a short life, but W. T. Westall—the St. Albans Goliath—took his place and Smalls' too. The second century was soon passed and the West Herts prospect, as bowler after bowler was tried, became blacker and blacker. However, when 70 more runs had been added A. A. Coles was caught third man by Barker, and the innings was declared closed, it being then 4 o'clock. Coles had by very fair cricket made 87, after about three hours' play, scoring eleven fours, five threes, seven twos, and fourteen singles. He was greatly aided by having a man to run for him. W. T. Westall, whose average should be high, as he carried out his bat for 82 against M.C.C., was not out 46. It should be mentioned that there were no less than 42 extras, 32 of which were byes. West Herts began their uphill fight at 4.15 by sending in White and G. R. Collins, Pearce and Sansom sharing the St. Albans attack. Collins was dismissed with the score at 18, he himself having made four runs, and Cobb came in and an advance was made to 39 before White was unfortunately given out lbw to Pearce. Eleven runs later Cobb was caught by Coles's substitute immediately after 50 had been telegraphed, being responsible for exactly half. R. G. Barnett and J. Woods only managed to break their ducks, and most of the scoring was done by Barker. He, however, found someone to help him when Astley came in, and over 80 runs had been added when the old Aldenhamian was dismissed by another catch by the substitute. The country was rased with Barker and Little together, but Barker was caught by Sansom when 186 had been reached. His 80 runs, a most useful combination, were the result of stylish though patient cricket. He was at the wickets for an hour and twenty minutes. Matters had now become very bad, and there was a quarter of an hour left for play, and only one wicket to fall. However, Little amply atoned for his shortcomings as wicket keeper, by keeping up his own wicket until 6.35 when stumps were drawn. The match was saved, and that was all, while it was decidedly hard for St. Albans. Score:—

WEST HERTS v. FROGMORE.
West Herts played the last of their home matches on Saturday against Frogmore. Play did not start until 2 o'clock, on account of the late arrival of the visitors. The Watford Club was able to put in a strong side, the only noticeable absence being White, who was playing against Norfolk the County. Frogmore had their usual eleven, and having won the toss W. M. Robertson and E. Gentle opened their attack. Gentle was caught by Little when he had made three, the Rev. F. Sullivan was bowled by Beaton for six, and Robertson was caught by a substitute off Astley's bowling for two runs, the result of a patient quarter of an hour's cricket, the first three wickets only producing 11 runs. Play became more exciting when F. May was joined by H. Hobbs, and the score was carried to 39 for the fourth wicket. Hobbs having contributed 12. W. T. Wright Millar dismissed with the last ball of his over, and on taking up again secured three more wickets in successive balls, the fifth and eighth wicket falling for 38. A. Wright, however, was not so easily sent back, and it was not until he had knocked up 11 that he was bowled by Burrows. May had until then been playing cautiously, but there being only one more wicket to fall he resolved to make hay while the sun shone, and before H. Cripps was bowled he had put together 81 runs. The innings closed, after many ups and downs for the fair score of 73. At twenty minutes to 4, W. R. Collins and F. E. Burrows went out for West Herts. None thought to win the match themselves. Burrows had to go back when he had made six, but a start was being made by Cobb and Collins, when the former unfortunately ran himself out with the score at 84. Very nearly half the runs had been scored, and there were eight more wickets to fall so that victory seemed certain. C. R. Humbert made a hit for three, but was caught by Westland, and later at 81 Collins was bowled for 22. After his departure the remaining batsmen collapsed, and Beaton, Astley, Millar, and Little were all dismissed without the addition of a single run, J. A. Murray's four and an extra brought the total up to 86, 10 runs behind the visitors. Only five batsmen failed to score. Frogmore went in again and did even better than before, as when stumps were drawn, with four wickets in hand they were only one short of the total for their first attempt. May was responsible for 19, and A. Wright was not out 11. The match was thus a win for Frogmore by 15 runs. Score:—
