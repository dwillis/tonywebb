The St. Albans Club had two matches only on Saturday, the 1st XI and the "A" team, meeting the 1st and 2nd elevens respectively of the Frogmore Club. On the former occasion when Frogmore tackled the Albanians, the latter just managed to win, but on Saturday the home side out-played their opponents at all points of the game, and want of time alone robbed them of victory.
* * *
Batting first the Albanians made a good show, scoring 178 for the loss of only three wickets, and of this number G. H. Small, who went in first, scored 101 and was not out when the innings was declared closed. This places Small at the top of the batting averages, a place held for so long by Field.
* * *
Rain had greatly interfered with the play, and it was late in the afternoon when Frogmore opened their venture. They played steadily, and when the stumps were drawn five wickets had fallen for 43, one of these Giddins, who was very fortunate, scored 25.
* * *
The visitors are to be heartily congratulated on their smart fielding. They gave very little away, and a large number of runs were saved which they might well have been excused for not preventing.
* * *
The game between the 2nd elevens was not a very interesting one. Frogmore could not raise a full team, and only nine men batted, their united efforts totalling 24. When the Albanians went to the wickets there was some tall hitting, and 117 runs were hit up without loss in forty minutes before rain induced the players to seek shelter.
* * *
During the next fortnight there will be cricket daily on Clarence Park, the St. Albans Club having arranged a Festival to extend over two weeks. Some capital matches have been arranged, and only two things are required to ensure the success of the undertaking—fine weather and big attendances.

ST. ALBANS v. FROGMORE.
St. Albans played the return match with Frogmore on Saturday on Clarence Park, the result being a draw in favour of the home team. Neither side was at full strength, and besides this the weather was very unfavourable, but the cricket was fairly attractive, the chief feature of interest being a fine not-out innings of 104 by G. H. Small, the captain of the St. Albans Club. Winning the toss the home side went to the wickets, Small and Westell being opposed by the deliveries of Boxall and Giddins. The ground was in wonderfully good condition after the recent rains, though the bowlers were slightly assisted by it. Off Boxall's second ball Westell was caught at third man by A. Wright, and the start was thus a sensational one—0-1-0. With the advent of Sansom, however, there was a great change. Some really good balls were sent up from both ends, and the fielding was remarkably smart and accurate, but the batsmen were bent on scoring, and runs came steadily, Small's cuts and off-drives and Sansom's drives being well worth watching. The partnership of these two was a long one, the score reaching 107 ere Sansom in attempting to drive Gentle gave a lofty one to Boxall at cover-point, which was neatly held, and the retiring batsman was applauded for a well hit 37. T. Pearce, who followed in, had a short stay, being taken at the second attempt by Robertson behind the wickets, but this was the extent of the success which the Frogmore bowlers met with, as Day and Small remained together until the innings was declared closed with the score at 178 for three wickets, though Day should have been caught at long leg off Boxall. Small made his runs in fine style, and was warmly applauded as he returned to the pavilion. With about an hour and a quarter to bat, Frogmore commenced their innings with Giddins and Boxall, the only thing for them to play for, being a draw, as it was out of the question for them to try to get the runs. Pearce and Sansom, as usual, were entrusted with the bowling, and only two runs were scored when Boxall was caught behind the wicket of Sansom. Giddins made one or two good hits, and then lost the company of W. Wright, who was snapped behind the sticks of Pearce, and Sargent, after making three, cut a ball from Sansom hard on to his wicket—20-5-3. Giddins, who had had some miraculous escapes, hit out freely when joined by Robertson, and added 20 runs ere the former's valuable but lucky innings was brought to a close with a catch behind the wickets, while shortly afterwards Robertson was smartly caught at point by Small off Pearce—43-5-10. As rain was steadily falling, and there was no chance of finishing the game stumps were drawn, the position of the game being greatly in favour of the home side. Score and analysis:
