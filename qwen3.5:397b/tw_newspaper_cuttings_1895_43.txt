CAMBRIDGE EXPRESS: SATURDAY 10 AUGUST 1895-2.

COUNTY v. BOROUGH POLICE.
The annual match between cricketing teams representing the Borough and County of Cambridge Police Forces took place on Wednesday, when the policemen journeyed to Royston and spent a pleasant time on the Heath. The County went first to the wicket, and made 50, to which p.c. F. Brooks largely contributed. The Borough team scored 68. Then followed a piece of good cricket, in which the brothers p.c.'s F. and G. Brooks hit away in dashing style, and they were so well supported by Inspector Webb and p.c. Carter that 99 runs for the loss of three wickets were registered quickly, after which stumps were drawn. The Borough thus obtained a victory on the first innings. The bowling was chiefly notable for p.c. Rackham's analysis of 6 for 15 runs for the Borough, and p.c. F. Brooks' 6 for 25 for the County. At the close of play the party sat down to lunch at the White Horse. The following is the score:—

SPORT AND PLAY.
GOSSIP UPON CURRENT SPORTS AND PASTIMES.
A well-known writer on sport sends me the following particulars of Kumar Shri Ranjitsinhji:—In a memorable cricket season the performances of K. S. Ranjitsinhji (as the famous Indian prefers to be known, instead of as Prince) have created almost as much attention as the doings of the great "W. G." "Ranji," as he is familiarly called in University cricket, came over from India some few seasons ago, and lived for a year or so with the Rev. Louis Borrisow, Precentor of Trinity College. In October, 1889, he matriculated as a Non-Coll. in the University of Cambridge, but, though taking to cricket, few opportunities presented themselves for displaying his abilities. In due course he entered the foundation of Trinity College, but excepting an occasional match for the 2nd Eleven he was little known in the cricket world until he became associated with Cassandra and other Town Clubs, when he immediately shot into prominence, and a great future was predicted for him. In the year 1892, neither Varsity nor College could be induced to recognise his sterling qualities. However, during that year he had a magnificent average, and totalled over 2,000 runs, topping the century on 10 or 11 different occasions.
* * *
From the commencement of 1893 season his exceptional prowess demanded the attention of the Varsity authorities, and Mr. Jackson awarded him his blue, but although he averaged 29.79 in Varsity matches, in the Oxford and Cambridge match he failed, as so many other great cricketers have done. That year he was a regular member of the Trinity 1st Eleven, and came out with the fine figures of 71 8, eclipsing the batting achievements of Messrs. Jackson, W. G. Druce, Wells, &c. His chief scores this season were 110, 111, 101, and 102 not out. He was appearing for the County of Cambs. in the '93 and '94 seasons, but from want of an extensive programme and other causes did not shine very conspicuously. He played in several first-class matches last season and came out eighth, with an average of 37.03 for 16 innings, and altogether scored six centuries, viz., 182, 146 not out, 137 not out, 122 not out, 115 not out, and 103 not out. While known but little in first-class matches, his services during last winter were sought by several counties, but having a residential qualification for Sussex he decided to throw in his lot with that county. It will suffice to record the fact that this season, up to August 3rd, he had totalled in all matches 2,117 runs, with an average of over 70, while in first-class matches up to the date mentioned he averaged 59.22, with an aggregate of 1,497, and the following centuries figure in this large total of 206 not out, 174 not out, 150, 137 not out, 110, and 100. The last four centuries are first-class. Space will not permit of further reference to his efforts with various other Clubs to which he belongs. On his day he is able to send down a good ball, and his early fame was established through his brilliant fielding at short slip.
* * *
K. S. Ranjitsinhji, born in September, 1872, is the second son of the Jaiu of Nawanagar, and is, therefore, in his 23rd year. His principal home in this country is Cambridge, where, during the winter months, he diligently participates in shooting and fishing. He is also a very good billiard player, and good judges have asserted that had he taken more keenly to tennis and racquets he would have represented his Varsity against Oxford in either pastime.
* * *
Ranjitsinhji, whose mode of living is an example which might well be followed by a number of cricketers, being a non-smoker and almost total abstainer, at the same time is one of the most jovial and unassuming fellows to meet; a keen supporter of all legitimate recreation, a severe critic on cricket, and very enthusiastic in all sport he indulges in.
* * *
Whether K. S. Ranjitsinhji returns to his native province at the end of this season remains to be seen. However, it is contended that he is too far infatuated and too great an exponent of cricket to be able to tear himself from a game in which he is so conspicuous at the present moment, and the wish of all sportsman is at least England will witness his prowess for many years to come.
* * *
The County of Cambs. Cricket Eleven cannot yet steer clear of rough luck, and had it been possible to complete the return match with M.C.C. and Ground, possibly they would have experienced considerable trouble in saving the game. Had the Marylebone Club batted under the same conditions as Cambs. they would not have made 211. The two Batters (win) are likely to be most useful for the County, Barnes' 118, and Devey batted well, especially the Notts. veteran. W. G. Grace, junr., who captured the M.C.C. eleven, bowled with success.

Shortly the final tie for the Cambs. Cricket Association Cup will be played, the contesting Clubs being the holders—the Old Higher Grade—and the Camden. Both Clubs command the respect of all votaries of the game, and the victory of either will be popular. The Camden, it is stated, have never given the Association any trouble, and have always been loyal to the Committee. They have frequently figured in the final stages, and are fully deserving of meeting with success. This equally applies to the "Old Boys," for whom P. Cornell has accomplished some truly wonderful feats with the ball of late, capturing six wickets for one run and seven for ten.
* * *
For them P. Luff is also a useful performer, and many think both he and Cornell should have received trials for the County. For the Camden O'Connor is the main cause for hope. Against Hunts. the county "pro." actually captured 11 wickets for 80 runs. Should he be able to repeat his best efforts the "Old Boys" would have anything but an easy task.
* * *
Several fairly good bats appear for each eleven, and everything points to an interesting game, and we hope the game will permit of the best side winning.
* * *
K. S. Ranjitsinhji informs me he will not be able to play any local cricket till after the Sussex programme is completed, when he hopes to be able to take part in afternoon matches.

The changes from the previous week were that A. E. Stoddart and G. Brann qualified for the first dozen, H. W. Bainbridge and H. K. Foster making way. The old Etonian, however, is the third Cambridge man to score 1,000 runs in first-class matches. Such a feat may have previously been accomplished, but I never heard of it, and F. S. Jackson was the only Cambridge man to reach those figures last year with 1020 for 39 innings. As N. F. Druce, Lord Hawke, Hayward, Carpenter, and F. S. Jackson have several more matches to play, it is quite possible they may join Messrs. Ranjitsinhji, Woods, and Bainbridge.
* * *
There is every prospect of Tom Hayward going out to South Africa with George Lohmann's team at the end of the season. I hear Tom is anxious to bring a team down to Cambridge at the end of the season. There was some talk of a match last April between K. S. Ranjitsinhji's XI. and Tom Hayward's. Such a fixture in September, with strong sides, should prove a great attraction.
