WORCESTERSHIRE CLUB AND GROUND v. BROMSGROVE.
There was a capital attendance at the above match played at Bromsgrove on Saturday for Rolleston, the professional's benefit, and a capital day's cricket was the result. Bromsgrove won the toss, but put their opponents in. Burrows and Bowley, who went in first, batted brilliantly, and made 155 before they were separated. With other useful and well played totals from W. S. Caldwell and the Rev. F. A. Bickmore, the large score of 258 was obtained, when the innings was declared with six wickets down. Bromsgrove observed strict defensive tactics, and kept their end up admirably, and only six wickets were down when the match ended in a drawn game. Score:-

CATHEDRAL GUILD v. MR. CONACHER'S XI.
Played at Diglis, on Saturday, and resulted in a decisive win for the Guild by 46 runs. The scratch eleven, winning the toss, elected to bat, but, with the exception of Mr. C. Riley, could make no stand against the bowling of S. Smith and Phillips, the former taking four wickets for 15, and the latter six for 21. The total absence of extras proved that Harford as the wicket-keeper was well up to his work. On going to bat, the Guild fared badly, no one reaching double figures till the eighth wicket was down, but Walker, Darch, and Morgan put a different complexion on the game, the captain playing extremely well for his 22. Mr. E. B. Porch, late of Malvern College, who plays for Hoomercs, is tenth in the batting averages of that club with an average of 16.11. He has scored 203 in 14 innings.

St. John's, in their match on Thursday at Boughton, against the Club and Ground, took a strong lead after going in first. They made a capital stand, although the bowling against them was rather warm. Burrows, Willoughby, and Ryder shared between them what wickets fell, before Mr. Young declared with nine wickets down. Mr. H. V. Plum batted very steadily for 48. Messrs. Outris and Mansell also performed creditably, and 123 was the total. This score looked like being wiped off, for Burrows was in his smiting humour and hit up 57 in lively fashion. The County lot were only 13 behind and six wickets down when time put a stop to proceedings.

Mr. E. P. Gem bowled the team very well, and I should think he has acted in a similar capacity before, although I have not known him do so.

It was rather funny that Straw should have been objected to by the St. John's captain, because I have an inkling that he has resorted to similar measures before, viz., when he has been short of players he has done the best he could to fill the vacancies. Although he had a right to object to Straw, who had not batted, I think he might have given way, but I suppose it was his customary zeal in trying to win the match that made him forget previous circumstances.

Mr. W. A. Young performed the hat trick.

Rolleston had his annual benefit at Bromsgrove on Saturday, and I hope he will have a substantial sum, for he is a very painstaking cricketer, and immensely popular with the members. He has done well for the club, which is second to none in the district.

I had a good opportunity of looking over the new pavilion, which is one of the best I have seen, and it reflects great credit upon Mr. Gadd, who, I was glad to hear, was getting another ready for the Handsworth Wood Club. Others will sure to follow, for at £115, the total cost, it represents the cheapest and most comfortable one that could be imagined.

The improved state of the ground struck me season as I got there. I subsequently learned that the brothers J. E. and E. Perkins, both of whom are independent gentlemen, set to work and levelled 14,000 feet with their own hands last winter. This substantiates what I have said of them before, that they are the right men in the right place. This is enthusiasm indeed, as those who have had a spell at that sort of work know very well. The wicket was a perfect one, and, after all the runs which were made, was just as good at the close.

I had forgotten to say that there is about £85 yet to be got to clear the pavilion debt, and I notice a match will be played there between 22 ladies and 11 gentlemen. This ought to wipe some of it off, for the tickets are going off well. If the weather is fine there will be a good gate and plenty of fun.

Burrows and Rowley established a record, for they hit up 155 before a wicket fell, and both batted splendidly. The former only just missed his century. Master Caldwell played a perfect innings, and this youngster has only to go on the right way to make a very fine batsman. I like his style very much. The Rev. F. A. Bickmore was batting first rate when the innings was declared. The score had reached 258 for six wickets, and this, against some good bowlers, was capital. I imagine the local team must have felt annoyed that they did not bat first after winning the toss. However, they played a good defensive game, and kept their end up for two hours and a quarter, and made a good draw of it.

Tommy Straw took off the gloves and went on to, well, I ought to say bowl, but the crowd and the home captain said he threw, and so I suppose he must have done. The circumstance caused a deal of flutter, and John Smith, who was umpiring, had a warm five minutes, but it all finished up in fun. I must warn Tommy to stick to his wicketkeeping, for he has few superiors in that line anywhere.

There are lots of local matches of an interesting kind during the coming week, and, given a continuance of the present glorious weather, will wind up a capital season.
