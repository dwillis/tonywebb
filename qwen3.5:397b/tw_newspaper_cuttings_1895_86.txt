OBSERVATIONS AND REMARKS ON THE CRICKET FIELD.
On Thursday last the rendezvous of those interested in good cricket, and who were at liberty, was the Constabulary Field, when a match was played between an eleven of amateurs chosen by Captain Nicholls and a team selected by Mr Bewicke. The chief object of the match was a laudable effort to bring together two of the best amateurs of the district, and to a very large extent it was realised. Disappointment was felt at the absence of Messrs R. Spencer, T. K. Inlison, C. F. Cumberland, M. Scott, and M. Fenwick; but the numerous spectators were fully compensated by the brilliancy of V. Simpson's hitting throughout his not out innings of 117. Such a display has not been seen in this county during the past quarter of a century, and it was remarked by habitues of Lord's and the Oval that they had never seen it excelled. The first-class play of V. Thompson (57) and Butler (62) was equally interesting, if not so exciting. The fielding on both sides, with one or two exceptions, was accurate and smart—far above the average of our club performances. The bowling was the weakest feature of the whole match, more especially on Mr Bewicke's side. In fact, the district does not possess a really good amateur bowler but G. Simpson. Such thrilling excitement was never before exhibited on a Northumberland cricket field, and it was most encouraging to hear so many express a wish that such a match should be repeated, more particularly as those entrusted at present with the resuscitation of county cricket are so dilatory and apathetic. At the close of the game Mr Bewicke immediately challenged Captain Nicholls to play him a two days' match at Close House, which, without hesitation, was conditionally accepted, viz., that the former agreement as to the exclusion of professionals should be faithfully adhered to, and that Mr Bewicke kept to his own list of amateurs. The dates fixed are in September, and it is a pity so few will have an opportunity of seeing it.
The news of the success attending the Borderers' recent tour in Scotland was hopefully received, and although nothing was known of the calibre of the teams selected to test their metal, the incidents of victory broke their unsaviable record of constant defeat, and might possibly give them a stimulus for more noble deeds at home.
It was mentioned that Capt. Nicholls's eleven plays the Northern Nomads on his ground on the 12th of this month, and that the Nomads would be a vastly superior lot to those who represented them last season. The teams will be under the management of Mr F. Clutton.
I also heard that Capt. Nicholls had invited Mr W. G. Grace to bring a team to this city. The champion graciously replied regretting he could not possibly do so this season, as he was fully engaged up to the middle of September. I understand the invitation will be repeated at the close of the year, and may it be successful.
A NORTHERN NOMAD.
