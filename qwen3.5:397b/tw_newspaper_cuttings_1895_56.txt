STOCKPORT CHRONICLE: MONDAY 26 AUGUST 1895-2.

SATURDAY'S MATCHES.
Stockport v. Cheadle Hulme.
Great interest was manifested in the meeting of these local rivals, which took place on the Cheadle Hulme ground in splendid weather and before a good attendance of spectators on Saturday afternoon. It was also set apart as the benefit of the Cheadle Hulme professional, Teather. The first match between these elevens this season ended in a very even draw. Skipper Hartley lost the toss, and of course had to field. O. V. Rome and Teather were the first batsmen to open the Cheadle innings to the bowling of Sam Brown and E. May. Teather scored a single off Brown, but next over Harry May touched his leg stump out of the ground. E. Rome came next, and immediately cut Brown for four, while O Rome got May away for a like number, but was out soon after, Brown taking him well at short slip. 12-2-8. Briggs joined E. Rome, but with an addition of eight runs on Rome was finely caught by Hartley after scoring 10. A. Hoyle was next in, and saw Briggs see Brown away to leg for four, and next over repeated the strike very luckily. Hoyle hit May for three, but soon after that player was magnificently caught by Harry May at short slip low down—a very fine catch indeed. (1) Taylor joined Briggs, and here a stand took place, though Briggs did most scoring. At 62, however, Fred Torrington, who had displaced May, clean bowled Briggs, that player having made 27 in not a very taking fashion; thus half the wickets were down for 63. W. Hadfern came next, but without any addition Fred Torrington upset his middle stump, with one of his expresses. Taylor left at 67 and Rome at 82, while Kirk left at 71, there being nine wickets down. Toon came a surprise for the Stockport men. Todd and Colles on getting together made things very lively for the fielders. Changes of bowling were tried, but to no use, the 100 going up amidst loud applause. Both men played a fine game, Todd several times getting the ball away in clean style. W. Hartley went on with lobs, but added no great sensation. When the total had reached 137 Colles was bowled by Fred Torrington, his innings of 15, coming at a time when things were looking serious, was of the utmost value to his side, while too much cannot be said of Todd, who carried out his bat for a splendid innings of 34. Certainly the Stockport men assisted them materially by their wretched fielding, but nevertheless they deserve great credit for their plucky partnership. Fred Torrington came out with a splendid analysis, out of 132 overs; he bowled six maidens for eight runs and three wickets. May had three for 25, and Brown three for 33. At ten minutes past five W. Hartley and J. Bates came out on behalf of Stockport, the bowling being entrusted to Teather and Hadfern. Bates cut Teather finely for four, while Hartley off-drove Hadfern soon after for a similar number. Runs came at a good pace. With the score at 24 Bates was bowled by a ball from Teather which came several inches across, and removed his leg ball; that player had scored fourteen in quick time. Arthur Torrington joined his captain, only to see him given out caught at the wicket, a decision which seemed doubtful. Brown came next, and here some of the finest cricket of the day was seen; both men hit out well. Still the bowling was good, but runs coming at a fair pace. Briggs duplicated Teather when the score had reached 72. Torrington was bowled for a splendid innings of 24, which included three fours and four twos. F. Tyler came next, but after scoring three was bowled. H. Hartley came next, and the first ball he received he drove it magnificently right out off the ground for four, this stroke quite taking the bowler by surprise. Sam Brown was out soon after from a big hit, while E. Rome took very well, after playing a grand game for 37, his innings included five fours and two twos. Disney came next and saw Hartley lay about both bowlers, Briggs coming in for a fair share of punishment, he getting that player away for two fours, but in attempting to repeat was caught at the wicket, his brilliant innings of 18 included three fours, one three, one two, and a single. He tried his very utmost to bring off a win, and indeed could he have stayed another quarter of an hour I believe his object would have been accomplished. Disney left at the same total, 103. With May and Selby in time was called, seven wickets being down for 110; thus the match ended in a draw in favour of the visitors. Below is appended full score and analysis:—
