NEWCASTLE DAILY JOURNAL: TUESDAY 4 JUNE 1895

CAPTAIN NICHOLL'S XI. v. MR J. A. BROOK'S XI.
One of the chief attractions at the Jesmond end of the city yesterday was the match between elevens raised by Captain Nicholls and Mr J. A. Brooks. The latter's team came from Carlisle, being composed of members of that club; and though not exactly the team which represents the Cumbrian organisation in their ordinary fixtures, was quite up to its usual strength.
The morning opened dull, and a slight shower of rain, following the storm of Saturday, made the pitch rather slow, and gave the bowlers great assistance. Thus the trundlers could get work on was quickly evident when once the game began, and all through the day the batsmen were compelled to watch the ball very carefully.
Captain Nicholl's team batted first, Tom Murphy and J G Wilson opening to the bowling of A Smith and McIlvena. The start was a disastrous one, Murphy being taken in the slips off a bumping ball which broke back, at 3; Wilson beaten by McIlvena at 10, and Butler also bowled at 16. Then H Haslam and Goodband, playing sound cricket, gradually carried the total to 93, before Goodband fell to a catch by McIlvena. The South Northumberland professional had played a free open game for 39. None of the succeeding batsmen could stay long with Haslam, who continued to score freely, and the whole side was out for 123. Haslam carried out his bat for a meritorious 61, in which he scarcely made a bad stroke, while his cutting was well-timed and hard.
A Smith and E Hodgson started for Carlisle after the luncheon interval; but Pye quickly disposed of the first named, clean bowling him for a "duck." With Jackson in, runs came freely, both men batting confidently. At 36 Goodband relieved Butler, and carried Hodgson's balls away with his first delivery. Downey and Todd quickly followed in and out, but J G Hodgson and Jackson made another good stand. At length, in trying to pull one from Goodband, Jackson got his leg in front of the wicket, and was out. His 40 was a capital display of cricket, for he played well all round, being equally as strong on the leg side as on the off; and he drove well along the ground. When Jackson fell the total stood at 99, and with the "century" up Irwin followed him out. Immediately afterwards J. G. Wilson missed Hodgson, and for a time it looked as if this mistake would cost the Tynesiders the match, but at 110 Pye beat Stordy, and two runs later got rid of McIlvena. Then Goodband joined in, and getting Quirk and Brooks for "blobs," finished off the innings for 115, leaving the home team victorious by eight runs. There were about 1,000 people on the ground during the afternoon. Score:—
