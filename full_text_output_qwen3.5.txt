Stockport v. Little Lever.
This match was played at Little Lever in splendid weather and before a large attendance of spectators. A start was not made till quite three o'clock owing to the Stockport men being delayed on the road. The match was set apart for the benefit of Perry, one of the Little Lever professionals, and this being his first year with them. Stockport still deplored the services of Fred Torkington and one or two others, while Little Lever had their strongest team in the field this season. Little Lever won the toss, and electing to bat, sent in Towler and Tyldesley to the bowling of Brown and F. Tyler. Tyldesley had a splendid reception on coming out—a fact due, no doubt, to that player being admitted to the Lancashire County ranks, and it will be remembered that on his second appearance for his county he scored no fewer than 153 not out against Warwickshire. Brown opened to Towler, beating him first ball, and the next ball bowled him leg stump. A. Smith came next, but after scoring six in a wretched fashion Brown upset his wicket. C. Smith, the Lancashire County wicket-keeper, joined Tyldesley, but immediately after Tyldesley was splendidly taken by Bates right on the boundary, and three good wickets were down for seven. Peel came next, and soon began to force matters, hitting Brown out of the ground for four. We may say the ground is a very small one indeed, it being no trouble to get a ball out. Smith seemed content with singles. The score rose steadily, and a change of bowling was tried at 30, Harry May displacing Tyler; the change almost worked immediately, May bowling Peel for a well hit 20. Hallows came next, and got May away to leg nicely for three, but at the other end Brown bowled him with a grand ball, and half the wickets were down for 45 runs. Simon joined Smith; the latter with his score at 16 was missed by Bates at long off. Simon was the next to go, Brown upsetting his wicket. 51—5—0. Knutton followed, and hit up 11 in four hits, but was then beautifully taken by Harold Hartley off Brown. 55—7—11. Northrop came next, and bore a little stand was made, Northrop getting May away for three while Smith cut the same bowler for a like number. The score had reached 81 when Brown disturbed Smith's wicket for a lucky innings of 32. A strange coincidence took place during Smith's innings, and one which certainly did not reflect much credit on the Little Lever umpire. W. Hartley, who had displaced May, was bowling his last ball of his second over, Smith hit the ball out to Bates at square leg and was caught, the umpire, in the meantime, saying "over," and when Bates had caught Smith the umpire give it a "no ball," and thus Smith was allowed to proceed with his innings. The end soon came, the innings closing for 91. Sam Brown deserves great credit for his splendid bowling, taking no fewer than eight wickets for 37 runs, hitting the stumps six times, while the other two were caught. Stockport opened their venture in a dreadful bad light. J. Bates and W. Hartley being the first batsmen to face Knutton and Penny. Bates after scoring a single was caught at the wicket. A. Torkington joined his captain, but after scoring five shared the fate of Bates. Brown came next, and hitting one out to Tyldesley was splendidly caught, and three wickets were down for 11 runs. Rain then commenced to fall, and put an end to all further play, and what would have been an interesting finish was spoiled. The Stockport team folded quite up to form. On Saturday next Werneth are down at Cale Green, and this match is set apart for the benefit of Sam Brown and Jim Dainty. As this is one of the best fixtures on the card and a fixture which takes well with the Stockport people, the two "pros." should materially benefit by it. Given a fine day a grand game is expected. CRICKET MATCH AT HAZEL GROVE.
A cricket match was played on the Hazel Grove Cricket Ground at Norbury on Monday afternoon, between Hazel Grove and Poynton. For several years past these two rival clubs have contested for supremacy, and considerable interest was taken in the match. In consequence of the annual Wakes a considerable number of people were enabled to be present and evinced a lively interest in the proceedings. The East Grove Brass Band was in attendance and played lively selections of music during the afternoon. During the earlier part of the afternoon the rain fell in torrents, and it was thought that the match would have to be abandoned. At three o'clock, however, the time fixed for the commencement of the match, the rain cleared off, and was succeeded by brilliant sunshine. Both elevens were fairly representative of the two clubs. Poynton, however, had a strong team, while Hazel Grove were short of the services of Gledhill, Penney, and Wood. The home captain won the toss and elected to bat. Cleaver and Fallows were the first to wield the willow, to the bowling of Rhodes and Clarke. Play was for a time rather dull, runs coming very slowly. Cleaver, the first man in, was neatly caught when he had succeeded in knocking up 8. Fallows' stay was very short, a solitary one falling to his lot; and Standring's career was cut short before he managed to break his duck. Roberts and Penney played well together, the latter especially batting steadily. Roberts, although he played a cautious game, was only successful in obtaining four runs, Wadsworth, with a neat catch, compelling him to relinquish his bat. Only about 30 runs had been scored at four o'clock, while five wickets had fallen, and the prospect for the home eleven began to look rather gloomy. Penney, who was playing a good game, was now partnered by Daniels, who lost no time in breaking his duck, and by united efforts these players speedily succeeded in raising the score marked indicator to 40. Daniels sent a beauty to leg for two, which was speedily followed by a three. His good luck, however, now came to an end, and the next ball took his stumps. The rest of the Hazel Grove team were quickly disposed of. Smith and Clayton were the first to take their stand against the bowling of Standring and Bennett. It was soon evident to the spectators that Poynton's chances of winning were rosy indeed. The Poynton lads batted well, and notwithstanding the good fielding of the homesters, quickly increased the score. Hazel Grove 53, Poynton 33. The top score for Poynton was 25, made by A. Clayton. W. Bailey came second with 24 to his credit.

Stockport v. Little Lever.
This match was played at Little Lever in splendid weather and before a large attendance of spectators. A start was not made till quite three o'clock owing to the Stockport men being delayed on the road. The match was set apart for the benefit of Penny, one of the Little Lever professionals, and one who has done splendid service with the ball, this being his first year with them. Stockport still deplored the services of Fred Torkington and one or two others, while Little Lever had their strongest team in the field this season. Little Lever won the toss, and electing to bat sent in Tawler and Tyldesley to the bowling of Brown and F. Tyler. Tyldesley had a splendid reception on coming out—a fact due, no doubt, to that player being admitted to the Lancashire County ranks, and it will be remembered that on his second appearance for his county he scored no fewer than 158 not out against Warwickshire. Brown opened to Tawler, beating him first ball, and the next ball bowled him leg stump. A. Smith came next, but after scoring six in a wretched fashion Brown upset his wicket. C. Smith, the Lancashire County wicket-keeper, joined Tyldesley, but immediately after Tyldesley was splendidly taken by Bates right on the boundary, and three good wickets were down for seven. Pool came next, and soon began to force matters, hitting Brown out of the ground for four. We may say the ground is a very small one indeed, it being no trouble to get a ball out. Smith seemed content with singles. The score rose steadily, and a change of bowling was tried at 30, Harry May displacing Tyler; the change almost worked immediately, May bowling Pool for a well hit 20. Hallows came next, and got May away to leg nicely for three, but at the other end Brown bowled him with a grand ball, and half the wickets were down for 45 runs. Simon joined Smith; the latter with his score at 15 was missed by Bates at long off. Simon was the next to go, Brown upsetting his wicket. 51—6—0. Keniton followed, and hit up 11 in four hits, but was then beautifully taken by Harold Hartley off Brown. 65—7—11. Northrop came next, and here a little stand was made, Northrop getting May away for three while Smith cut the same bowler for a like number. The score had reached 81 when Brown disturbed Smith's wicket for a lucky innings of 32. A strange coincidence took place during Smith's innings, and one which certainly did not reflect much credit on the Little Lever umpire. W. Hartley, who had displaced May, was bowling his last ball of his second over, Smith hit the ball out to Bates at square leg and was caught, the umpire, in the meantime, saying "over," and when Bates had caught Smith the umpire give it a "no ball," and thus Smith was allowed to proceed with his innings. The end soon came, the innings closing for 91. Sam Brown deserves great credit for his splendid bowling, taking no fewer than eight wickets for 37 runs, hitting the stumps six times, while the other two were caught. Stockport opened their venture in a dreadful bad light, J. Bates and W. Hartley being the first batsmen to face Knutton and Penny. Bates after scoring a single was caught at the wicket. A. Torkington joined his captain, but after scoring five shared the fate of Bates. Brown came next, and hitting one out to Tyldesley was splendidly caught, and three wickets were down for 11 runs. Rain then commenced to fall, and put an end to all further play, and what would have been an interesting finish was spoiled. The Stockport team failed quite up to form. STOCKPORT WIN BRILLIANTLY.
This match between these old rivals was played at Oak Green Park in magnificent weather, and before a large crowd of spectators, it being also set apart for the benefit of the professionals, Brown and Disney, who throughout the season have done splendid work, and it is hoped that they will receive a substantial reward. It will be remembered that the first time these teams met at Werneth this season Stockport won a most interesting match by sixteen runs, due on the whole to the splendid bowling of the evergreen Sam Brown. On Saturday skipper Hartley won the toss, and of course chose to bat, on a splendid wicket, taking in with him J. Bates to face the bowling of Hall and Hodgkinson. The start was none too good from a Stockport point of view, as Bates in trying to get a full toss from Hodgkinson to leg was elegantly caught at slip. Arthur Torkington joined his captain who cut Hall splendidly for four, but after scoring ten he was caught at the wicket. Sam Brown came next and was warmly greeted by the crowd. He immediately got to work, scoring runs faster than Torkington. The score had reached 37 when Torkington was caught by Redfern, after that player had scored six. Disney joined his brother professional but only to see him brilliantly caught at short slip by Cullison, after playing a splendid game. H. R. Bromley-Davenport of Cheshire county and Cambridge University fame came next and here a splendid stand took place, both men defying all the efforts of the Werneth bowlers. Davenport delighted the spectators with his batting, while Disney, on the other hand, was playing a most finished game, his cutting being a treat. The spectators seemed to fairly enjoy the pleasant sport, and especially so to see their own men come out on the right side.

CRICKET.
BY UBIQUE.
RESPECTED SIR,—Far be it from me to deny, or even deprecate, the gingerbread-making qualities of that marvellous place, Ormskirk; but, sir, I must gently but firmly draw the line at what the Ormskirk natives glorify by the name of cricket. Cricket, forsooth! I—Eh? Where's Ormskirk? You don't mean to say you never heard of Peerless Ormskirk? Dear, dear! What appalling ignorance! Why, you can find it on the map with a microscope and a week to spare—
Oh! Ormskirk is a marvellous spot;
A marvellous spot it be.
And woe to the man who says it's not:—
(With bullets of gingerbread he'll be shot)—
For the natives can point on the map to a blot
As big as the eye of a flea.
And it boasts a standing population of a cricket (?) team, seven enthusiastic spectators, and a small dog. Oh! and also a gent. what scores for the team and makes speeches with an average of 45 perorations about the paralysing miracles of batting with which the players weekly astound the world as it stares gaping and transfixed with wonder and awe. What's that? Well, if you don't believe me ask the select audience which was grouped within and around the scoring box last Saturday, and which went black in the face with suppressed laughter as the Petrifying Puffer held forth during the progress of the fiasco between Ormsquirk and the Park.
Yes, sir! Fiasco is the right and only term to apply to the occasion,—none other genuine. For I do not speak without serious consideration when I protest that of all the many and various samples of the noble art of batting I have seen, never—I emphatically repeat—never have I experienced so insufferably feeble and unworthy an exhibition as that of Ormskirk on the Park's ground last week end. Faugh! I scarce have patience to think of it;—of the hundreds at the match not one spectator with a spark of manliness or sportsmanship in him, could look at the farce without a sneer of contempt or a disgusted exclamation. And the remarks of the scornful crowd were numerous and varied, but very much to the point. But let me rouse your indignation by a short description of the thing. In the first place, to show there was no ill-feeling, the Park lost the toss; which, considering Ormskirk's somnolent methods, was fatal. For when Ormskirk goes to the wickets and begins to "bat," the spectators are liable to fall into a refreshing sleep. Though, to speak fairly and give them their due, I have known an Ormskirk player, under favourable circumstances, to make a two hit! But one cannot always expect such a feat.
Well, the gingerbread purveyors, with great assiduity commenced to block every ball that toddled up the pitch and the scoring for an hour averaged upwards of one run to each over! But what price this,—Ormskirk took an hour to score their first 50 runs!? We had to pour cold water down the Park-scorer's neck to keep him awake,—he wasn't used to it. All sorts of bowlers were tried, but it was block, block, block, and nothing came of it, but frantic yells of delight from the crowd when one of the stone-waller's scored a single! Which was a rare occurrence. As time dragged wearily on, and maiden overs were painfully fashionable, I could stand it no longer, and went for a walk to have a quiet swear to myself. When I returned—joy! the last Ormskirk stick in the mud was bowled, and I then discovered that the whole eleven had positively and actually taken up 3 (three!) hours in scoring a total of 149 runs! I muttered a prayer of thanksgiving that it was all over, and washed it down with a No. 9 Pipkin.
You may bet your respectability that Holden and Co. were just itching to lay their hands on the bat, and show these timid Ormskirkers how runs were made. And, by the Hokey!—but stay; all in good time. As the gingerbread gents had very carefully contrived to leave the Park three-quarters of an hour in which to bat, our premiers could not expect to give their opponents the all-powerful thrashing they administered at the last meeting. So Ormskirk had gained their end. Well, Holden and Ould opened the cannonade, and I may say at once that the captain's phenomenal display of gigantic hitting has rarely, if ever, been equalled on the Park ground,—which is a large order! Bennion and Barnes were the trundlers to suffer. I wept for them. Thirty-four runs in five overs! Four upon four came from Cecil's wonderful strokes, and at one time he followed up four successive boundary hits by swiping the ball clean over the pavilion for 6! What a paralysing contrast to Ormskirk's senile pottering at the crease! For, though but 30 minutes at the wickets Holden piled up 51 runs, which, with the exception of five singles, was one mass of 4's with a 6 thrown in for the sake of variety. With apologies to "The Man that Struck O'Hara,"—

First he spenked 'em through the crowd,
Then he baulked 'em where he chose;
Then he had another go—
Smiting sixes fours and twos!
Such a sight you never saw—
(Ormskirk but for time would lose)
Rat-tat-tat!
A whirling bat,
A high-sailed ball
Was left—that was all—
Of poor Ormskirk's gruelled bowling!
And the sanguine scorer who made speeches said "Well, I never did!" and tried to change the subject. When time arrived the Park had totalled 75 in three quarters of an hour, and having six wickets in hand, nothing come of it—to the visitors' relief. Of course, had there been time to win the home eleven would have adopted steadier tactics, but Cecil always knows when to do the right thing, more power to him. And in awakening the crowd sleeping under the influence of the Ormskirk-narcotic, he did the greatest thing of all. Ormskirk! Bah! I'd strike 'em off the fixture list, eh W—?
When Oxton tried conclusions with Huyton, at Oxton, on Saturday, a Liverpool cricketer remarks, "small scoring was the order." Of course, it's purely a matter of taste, but though "small scoring" was the order for Oxton and Huyton, some degraded people would prefer their order: to be small Scotch! Not too small, but just lemon enough. [The Local Voice will please excuse the culpable outburst of Humanity.] Huyton, batting first, fell away, so to speak, on the spot. Their experience was indeed a sad one, for the Bowling-Bishop and Smith relieved them of their duties by the time the total had hobbled up by jerky stages to 55. Oxton smiled largely as they proceeded to knock 'em off. But Oxton are sanguine persons, and you never can tell. For our suburbans manifested an alarming propensity for hatching "ducks," and wanted—very badly!—about 10 runs to win when the ninth wicket went by the board. But—dear old "but"!—amid tremulous excitation and agitated gaspings, P. Brownell and W. Scott saw the sinking ship safe through the breakers into harbour (pardon the simile), and when the last man was "petted" Oxton had reached the winning post first by 7 runs. Those similes have got a bit mixed up somehow, so we had better escape to Aigburth.
At Aigburth, on Saturday, Rock Ferry were busy replying to Liverpool's challenge. But they were more busy with the ball than the bat, for the castigation they had to submit to covered the whole ground, and so did the South End fielders, many times over. People said that after such a thrashing the hearts of the Rocks would be pained and sore, but all practical persons will agree with me that after such a "burst" of fielding, the soreness would be located in their feet. I merely offer this as an 'umble opinion, and as a guarantee of good faith. However, Fax is what we're hankering after. Rock Ferry opening the batting, immediately parted with the wickets of Kripps, Ravenscroft, and A. Stott, two of whom would get their l.b.w. It was a bad business. This ominous decay was delayed by Needam (32) and T. R. Bretherton (15), but the remainder of the eleven, "not to be denied," had a little rot "on their own," which was so successful that the grand total recorded was 67. Not very dazzling, was it? By the way, Needham was also out l.b.w. Three leg-before decisions are enough to drive an eleven into an asyl—er—hotel. Minus the discomfort of having to face E. C. Ryland—who would have done them a world of good on such a wicket—Liverpool enjoyed themselves, as I have hinted, and the fun waxed fast and—faster (Ha, ha! caught you tripping there, eh?) and when hostilities ceased they had piled up 174 for 7 wickets. The only South End bowler to make an impression was A. Stott, who captured 6 of the 7 wickets for only 39 runs. If Ryland had been there to help him, who can tell what—but "ifs" are idle now!
As I witnessed part of the contest between Victoria and New Brighton, at the Park, on Saturday, I certainly expected the Vics to annex what I thought would be a well-merited triumph. Instead of which they were beaten by 31 runs, so that New Brighton have avenged the unhappy downfall they underwent when the season was young. A trifle more of that estimable cricketing virtue, consistency, would have given the homesters the palm, for they could unfortunately only produce three double-figurers, viz.—J. McDonald—the substantial old warrior—(48), J. Wylde (23), and G. Lumgair (16). Only this and nothing more. And I would respectfully inquire of such hitters as W.T. J. Brocklebank, Tom Fish, &c., why this unwarrantable thunsness? Now, on the other hand, the visitor's distribution of duty gives one a sensation of satisfaction when looking at their list, as the double-figurists were seven strong. Yes, "we are seven."
At last! our Police 1st eleven have had to knuckle under and sing exceedingly small; though Bromboro Pool on Saturday surely "made them dance." The contest, which was witnessed at the Pool, was a surprise and no mistake. In this way. The home eleven—who are famously erratic in mixing up,—

Great deeds, and doings small,
Other deeds and none at all!
with the assistance of J. Pugh (44 not out), and the Rev. F.B. (20), totted up 123. The boys in "white" promised to cover this in double-quick time, A. Wright and J. Howard—each 20 runs—commencing the victors' retaliation in brisk fashion. But the ways of the Bromboro Bowlers are curious and inscrutable, and lo and behold! likewise ecco! not another policeman could stand before them; the remaining 9 rotted to a man, and the constables' feelings were hurt when they suddenly discovered that they were beaten by 33 runs!

BIRKENHEAD NEWS: SATURDAY 14 SEPTEMBER 1895 - 2.

CRICKET NOTES.

Saturday saw the practical conclusion of the season, the leading local clubs all finishing their list of engagements. The Premier club should have played Huyton, but as the latter's ground was nothing short of a quagmire after the heavy rain of the previous evening, the match had perforce to be abandoned. It is some time back now since the Parkites have had such a successful season as the one just concluded, as their position at the head of the district competition readily testifies. Independent, however, of their excellent record against the district clubs, they have done more than well against such powerful opponents as those of Manchester, Leyland and Wigan, the latter being beaten in both their engagements with the Park club. It will be seen on reference to the batting and bowling averages that T. R. Hubback heads the list in both departments of the game, a position which indicates what an invaluable acquisition he has been to his club with both bat and ball, independent of the great service he has rendered behind the wicket. R. Fielding Ould has secured the second position in the batting list, being closely followed by H. J. Fish, who, owing to a severe illness has been prevented from taking part in several important matches. On the whole skipper Holden can hardly be said to have played up to the form exhibited by him for several previous years, and it looks a trifle curious to see his name so low down in the list as fourth, but still an average of 21.11 for 26 innings is by no means bad, and besides this he has rendered excellent service with the ball, having captured 45 wickets at an average cost of 9.20. Indeed he has seldom bowled better than he has done this season, and he is to be congratulated on his capital record with the sphere. H. M. Grayson comes out fairly well with an average of 19.1 for 16 innings, but Smith shows a great falling off as compared with last year, his figures being 16.3 for 19 ventures. Town has done a little better, he having an average of 18 for 13 innings, whilst others who figure rather low down, but who may be confidently expected to do much better in the future, include H. W. P. Bennette, H. Salmon, and H. S. Cochrane. That good old stonewaller, A. B. Wilson, does not appear to have exercised his usual discretion, for he comes very near to being the "absolute," this unenviable position being occupied by Captain Byrne. The brunt of the bowling has of course being borne by Smith, who has taken 88 wickets for 10.9, whilst O. E. Steen's 48 have been secured at a rate of 13.7. Town has had a singularly unfortunate season, as although he has bowled uniformly well he has been able to get but few wickets, 24 at a cost of 22.11 being rather expensive.

The Rock Ferrys concluded a fairly successful season on Saturday with a win over Cheadle Hulme. Of the 25 matches they have played, 11 have been won, 6 drawn, and 7 lost, whilst one had the almost unique result of a tie. A glance at the batting averages shows E. F. Kearsey at the head of affairs with the nice figures of 20.88 for nine innings, and it seems rather a pity that his services are not more often available for first team matches. A. J. Cripps comes next, but his average compares unfavourably with that of Kearsey, there being a considerable difference between 20.88 and 12.23, albeit Cripps has played two more innings. With the exception of his captain, A. Stott, who is placed third, has taken part in more matches than any other player in the team, and his 21.13 for 24 innings must be set down as distinctly creditable. G. G. Paton has an average of 20.4 for 14 innings, whilst immediately following him comes "Capting" Ravenscroft who, on the whole, has been far from fortunate, repeated failures reducing his figures to as low as 19.79 for 26 innings. Another batsman who has not shown to great advantage this season is T. R. Bretherton, whose figures of 18.78 for 20 attempts are not as good as might be expected from such a capable willow wielder, whilst the average of A. C. Blain is also a little disappointing, this usually remarkably vigorous batsman being comparatively low down in the list with 18.33 for 18 innings. Needham's figures are 17.28 for 23 attempts, the next in order of merit being A. C. Bamford, who for 16 innings has an average of 14.46. Surprisingly low down is H. S. Bamford who, however, has not devoted much time to the game, having only been at the wickets on six occasions, for which he has an average of 10.14. J. Bretherton, E. Stott, G. B. Paterson, and E. C. Rylands are also allotted places in the list, the last being Ryland who, out of 14 innings, has been not out no less than seven times, and would probably have had a much higher average than 14 if he had been given a chance of showing his abilities with the bat. If he has not proved himself a brilliant batsman, however, there can be no question about his success with the ball, for during the season he has captured no fewer than 113 wickets at the moderate rate of 11.03 per wicket. J. Bretherton, though bowling well, falls far behind Ryland, the fast bowler only crediting himself with 83 wickets at a cost of 12.36. With such strong amateur bowling talent at its disposal, the club was not required to call upon the services of Needham to any great extent, but the pro has been very useful at times, and has secured 26 wickets at an average cost of 17.19.

Though the Oxtonites have been under a cloud for the last two or three weeks, they cannot be said to have fared badly on the whole, as out of 28 matches played they have won 9 and drawn 13, being absolutely beaten on but five occasions, one, the match referred to in my remarks about Rock Ferry, resulting in a tie. On Saturday, at Aigburth, they met with another serious reverse, receiving a shocking trouncing at the hands of the Liverpool representatives, who disposed of them for 71, and knocked up 133 for the loss of seven wickets. The Oxtonites thus ended the season with three successive losses. Their batting averages reveal the fact that M. Court has not only scored the most runs, but he has also the best average, his figures being 32.67 for 27 innings, a really excellent record for this very promising young player. The second position on the list is filled by J. H. P. Grayson, with 27.87 for nine innings, but now that this undoubtedly fine batsman has settled down and definitely thrown in his lot with the Oxtonites, we may expect to see a marked improvement in his figures next year. Another old Park player takes third honours, this being W. Turner, who has developed into one of the mainstays of the Oxton batting, this being evident by his excellent average of 26.67 for 25 innings. The Oxton skipper, C. F. Hutton has been a little off colour occasionally during the season, and has not scored quite so prolifically as he has done in former years, his highest contribution in an innings being 52, and his average 20.38 for 15 ventures. That the new pro. Bishop will be a great acquisition to the Oxtonites next season there can be but little doubt, for during the short time he has been engaged he has done wonderfully well with both bat and ball, having a batting average of 19.29 for 9 innings, whilst with the ball he has secured 48 wickets at a cost of 9.4 per wicket. J. Curwen has scored better and more consistently than he has done for a considerable time, and his record of 19.12 for 20 innings indicates that his powers as a batsman have not yet altogether left him. H. T. Hilbert has only taken part in 12 matches, for which his figures are 16.17, whilst for 19 innings M. Field has an average of 14.22. Through an injury received early on in the season, Smith was unable to take as active a part in the campaign as could be wished, and for his 12 innings he is rather low down on the list with 13.52. H. Bickersteth has played in ten matches, and has scarcely been so successful in taking out his bat as he has been in former seasons, consequently his average has suffered somewhat, and his figures of 10.86 are not calculated to make him unduly conceited. G. H. Court, B. S. Smith, and P. L. Bromnall are all close together with figures ranging from 10.33 to 8.18. L. Court bringing up the rear with 3.86 for 2 innings. Bowling honours are divided between amateurs and professionals, Bishop coming on well with 41 wickets at a cost of 9.4. Smith has proved a trifle more expensive his figures being 63 for 9.62, but his record is an excellent one for all that, more especially in view of the accident he met with, before referred to. G. H. Court has also rendered splendid service with the ball, capturing no less than 49 wickets, whilst his younger brother L. Court has given signs of future usefulness by securing 12 wickets for 3.86 per wicket.

Like that of Oxton, the Birkenhead Victoria first string have been faring badly of late, and also like Oxton they concluded their campaign on Saturday with a disastrous defeat, the Bootle representatives, who have done anything but well themselves this year, achieving an easy victory over the evidently disorganised Victorians, who made a very feeble show with the bat, and were all sent to the rightabout for 71. On the whole the Victoria representatives can hardly be congratulated on their season's record, for out of 25 matches played they have only won four, whilst 9 have been lost and 12 drawn. It is not surprising to see J. Wylde at the head of the batting averages with 25.14, for he has shown consistent good form throughout the season. Others who may be said to have done well are W. T. Davies (22.14), F. Wright (19.4) W. H. Routledge (18.7) and J. Macdonald (17.5), but a falling off is noticeable in the batting of J. Brooklebank, who is very low down on the list with 14.6, though he has played in more matches than any of his colleagues. J. Fish has been a distinct acquisition with the leather and heads the bowling averages with 27 wickets for a trifle over 11 runs per wicket. So far as taking wickets is concerned, however, J. Brooklebank is an easy first, he having captured 42 at a cost of 14.32. Several others of the team have had a trial with the ball, these including J. West, S. Wright, M. Sprat, J. Wylde, E. H. Carter, and W. H. Routledge.
